Title

 Attention Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder 

Sub Title

 314.0x 

Proposed Revision

SUMMARY OF CHANGES UNDER CONSIDERATION FOR ADHD

Please see DSM-5 Options Being Considered for ADHD for details of arguments for and against specific changes.

   

A. General Structure (reasons and risks/benefits listed in the Rationale tab): Two options are being considered: 1) Maintain present criteria but without subtypes and with only a single diagnostic code. Provide scales to designate behavior-specific features; or 2) Discontinue predominantly hyperactive/impulsive and predominantly inattentive subtypes. The ADHD should be assigned to the criteria currently used to designate the combined subtype. Create a new diagnosis of “Attention-Deficit Disorder” (ADD) with its own code (see B2 below).

B. Changes in Coding Inattention (see Rationale tab): Several of the proposals for the general structure alter the coverage of inattention. Two proposals have been tabled: 1) Retain the current predominantly inattentive subtype but require that no more than 2 hyperactivity criteria contribute to the diagnosis. 2) Create a new ADD diagnosis with the same inattention criteria as are present in DSM-IV predominantly inattentive subtype requiring that no hyperactive/impulsivity criteria are present.

C. Number of Criteria (see Rationale tab): Add 4 new impulsivity criteria to provide even coverage of hyperactivity/impulsivity and inattention.

D. Age of Onset (see Rationale tab): 1) Broaden the age of onset from “on or before age 7” to “on or before age 12.” 2) Change indicator of onset from impairment to symptom.

E.  Specification of the Disorder for Adults (see Rationale tab): Reduce criteria needed for any subtype from 6 to 3.

F.  Ascertainment of Cross-Situationality (see Rationale tab): Require that cross-situationality be based on direct report to the current informant or to the clinician by teacher, employer, or significant other.

G. Wording of Criteria (see specifics in Rationale tab): Provide more elaborate descriptions of diagnostic criteria.

H. Exclusion Criteria (see Rationale tab): No longer regard autism-spectrum or pervasive developmental disorder as excluders.

Rationale
Please see DSM-5: Options Being Considered for ADHD for considerations regarding changes to the diagnostic criteria for attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder.

Severity
Recommendations for severity criteria for this disorder are forthcoming. We encourage you to check our Web site regularly for updates.

DSM-IV
Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

A. Either (1) or (2):

(1)  six (or more) of the following symptoms of inattention have persisted for at least 6 months to a degree that is maladaptive and inconsistent with developmental level:

Inattention

(a)  often fails to give close attention to details or makes careless mistakes in schoolwork, work, or other activities

(b)  often has difficulty sustaining attention in tasks or play activities

(c)  often does not seem to listen when spoken to directly

(d)  often does not follow through on instructions and fails to finish schoolwork, chores, or duties in the workplace (not due to oppositional behavior or failure to understand instructions)

(e)  often has difficulty organizing tasks and activities

(f)   often avoids, dislikes, or is reluctant to engage in tasks that require sustained mental effort (such as schoolwork or homework)

(g)  often loses things necessary for tasks or activities (e.g., toys, school assignments, pencils, books, or tools)

(h)  is often easily distracted by extraneous stimuli

(i)   is often forgetful in daily activities

(2)  six (or more) of the following symptoms of hyperactivity/impulsivity have persisted for at least 6 months to a degree that is maladaptive and inconsistent with developmental level:

Hyperactivity

(a)  often fidgets with hands or feet or squirms in seat

(b)  often leaves seat in classroom or in other situations in which remaining seated is expected

(c)  often runs about or climbs excessively in situations in which it is inappropriate (in adolescents or adults, may be limited to subjective feelings of restlessness)

(d)  often has difficulty playing or engaging in leisure activities quietly

(e)  is often “on the go” or often acts as if “driven by a motor”

(f)   often talks excessively

Impulsivity

(g)  often blurts out answers before questions have been completed

(h)  often has difficulty awaiting turn

(i)   often interrupts or intrudes on others (e.g., butts into conversations or games)

B. Some hyperactive-impulsive or inattentive symptoms that caused impairment were present before age 7 years.

C. Some impairment from the symptoms is present in two or more settings (e.g., at school [or work] and at home).

D. There must be clear evidence of clinically significant impairment in social, academic, or occupational functioning.

E.  The symptoms do not occur exclusively during the course of a Pervasive Developmental Disorder, Schizophrenia, or other Psychotic Disorder and are not better accounted for by another mental disorder (e.g., Mood Disorder, Anxiety Disorder, Dissociative Disorder, or a Personality Disorder).

Code based on type:

314.01 Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder, Combined Type: if both Criteria A1 and A2 are met for the past 6 months

314.00 Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder, Predominantly Inattentive Type: if Criterion A1 is met but Criterion A2 is not met for the past 6 months

314.01 Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder, Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive Type: if Criterion A2 is met but Criterion A1 is not met for the past 6 months

Coding note: For individuals (especially adolescents and adults) who currently have symptoms that no longer meet full criteria, “In Partial Remission” should be specified.
