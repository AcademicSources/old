Title

 Rumination Disorder 

Sub Title

 307.53 

Proposed Revision

Updated October-6-2010

The work group has proposed that this diagnosis be reclassified from Disorders Usually First Diagnosed in Infancy, Childhood, or Adolescence to Eating Disorders

DSM-5 Proposed Diagnostic Criteria for Rumination Disorder

A. Repeated regurgitation of food over a period of at least 1 month. Regurgitated food may be re-chewed, re-swallowed, or spit out.

B. There is no evidence that an associated gastrointestinal or other general medical condition (for example, gastroesophageal reflux) is sufficient to account alone for the repeated regurgitation.

C. The eating disturbance does not occur exclusively during the course of Anorexia Nervosa, Bulimia Nervosa, or Binge Eating Disorder, or Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder. 

D. If the symptoms occur in the context of another mental disorder (for example, Mental Retardation or a Pervasive Developmental Disorder), they are sufficiently severe to warrant independent clinical attention.

Rationale

Rationale 

It is recommended that Rumination Disorder be moved from ‘Disorders Usually First Diagnosed in Infancy, Childhood, or Adolescence’ and into ‘Eating and Feeding Disorders’ as it can occur across the age range.

Level of change: Clarification.

References: Literature review (Bryant-Waugh et al., in press).

 

Criterion A: 

The deletion of ‘and rechewing’ and the addition of ‘Regurgitated food may be re-chewed, re-swallowed, or spit out’ are recommended to take account of documented evidence that not all patients (particularly older patients of normal intelligence) rechew regurgitated food.” In addition ‘following a period of normal functioning’ has been deleted as in practice this may be difficult to ascertain.

Level of change: Clarification.

References: Literature review (Bryant-Waugh et al., in press).

 

Criterion B:

The wording ‘is not due to’ has been changed to:  ‘There is no evidence that an associated gastrointestinal or other general medical condition (e.g., esophageal reflux) is sufficient to account alone for the repeated regurgitation’ as patients with rumination disorder may have a history of reflux and it is in practice often difficult to reliably separate out medical and psychological components of presentation.

Level of change: Clarification.

References: (Bryant-Waugh et al., in press)

 

Criterion C:

Binge Eating Disorder has been added to maintain parallel with Anorexia Nervosa and Bulimia Nervosa. It is well documented that rumination occurs in association with other eating disorders.

Level of change: Clarification.

References: Literature review (Bryant-Waugh et al., in press).

 

Criterion D:

The two parts of Criterion C of Rumination Disorder in DSM-IV have been separated out to retain consistency with proposed criteria for Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder. ‘During the course of’ has been changed to ‘in the context of another mental disorder’ to retain consistency with parallel criteria for Pica and Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder and because the two examples given (Mental Retardation and PDD) do not really run a course.

Level of change: Clarification.

References: (none – based on Work Group discussion and consensus).

 

Literature Cited:

Bryant-Waugh R, Markham L, Kreipe RD, et al.: Feeding and Eating Disorders in Childhood. Int J Eat Disord, in press.

 

Severity
 

Severity Criteria:

Severity of medical or psychosocial problems associated or potentially associated with the disorder:

0:            None

1:            Mild (subthreshold)

2:            Meets diagnostic criteria

3:            Moderate

4:            Severe

DSM-IV
 

Rumination Disorder

A. Repeated regurgitation and rechewing of food for a period of at least 1 month following a period of normal functioning.

B. The behavior is not due to an associated gastrointestinal or other general medical condition (e.g., esophageal reflux).

C. The behavior does not occur exclusively during the course of Anorexia Nervosa or Bulimia Nervosa. If the symptoms occur exclusively during the course of Mental Retardation or a Pervasive Developmental Disorder, they are sufficiently severe to warrant independent clinical attention.
