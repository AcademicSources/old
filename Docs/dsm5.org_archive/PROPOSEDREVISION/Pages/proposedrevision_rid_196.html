M 02 Narcolepsy/Hypocretin Deficiency

Narcolepsy/Hypocretin Deficiency

Updated May 1, 2012

A.     Recurrent periods of irrepressible need to sleep, lapses into sleep or naps occurring within the same day. These must have been occurring at least three times per week over the last 3 months (when individual is untreated).

B.     The presence of at least two of the following:

           1.     Cataplexy defined as: (i) brief (seconds to minutes) episodes of sudden bilateral loss of muscle tone with maintained consciousness, precipitated by laughter or joking in individuals with long standing disease, or (ii) spontaneous grimaces or jaw opening episodes with tongue thrusting or a global hypotonia without any obvious emotional triggers in children and/or in individuals within 6 months of onset. These episodes must occur at least a few times per month (when the individual is untreated for this symptom).

           2.     Hypocretin deficiency, as measured using cerebrospinal fluid hypocretin-1 immunoreactivity measurements (<1/3 of values obtained in healthy subjects tested during the same assay, or ≤110 pg/ml). Low cerebrospinal fluid hypocretin-1 must not be observed in the context of acute brain injury, inflammation or infection.

           3.     Nocturnal sleep polysomnography showing rapid eye movement (REM) sleep latency less than or equal to 15 minutes, or a Multiple Sleep Latency Test showing a mean sleep latency less than or equal to 8 minutes and two or more sleep onset REM periods.

 

Rationale for the Proposed Changes to Narcolepsy/Hypocretin Deficiency

Updated May 1, 2012

Summary:  The Sleep Disorders Workgroup recommends the following:

Replacing “narcolepsy” by “narcolepsy/hypocretin deficiency”

Rewording criteria A “Irresistible attacks of refreshing sleep that occur daily over at

least 3 months” as “Recurrent periods of irrepressible need to sleep, lapses into sleep or naps occurring within the same day. These must have been occurring at least three times per week over the last 3 months (when individual is untreated).”

In the original classification, only one of two B criteria had to be met. In the revised classification, two of 3 B criteria have to be met.

Rewording criteria B1 “cataplexy (i.e., brief episodes of sudden bilateral loss of muscle tone, most often in association with intense emotion)” as  “Cataplexy defined as: (i) brief (seconds to minutes) episodes of sudden bilateral loss of muscle tone with maintained consciousness, precipitated by laughter or joking in individuals with long standing disease, or (ii) spontaneous grimaces or jaw opening episodes with tongue thrusting or a global hypotonia without any obvious emotional triggers in children and/or in individuals within 6 months of onset. These episodes must occur at least a few times per month (when the individual is untreated for this symptom).”

Delete criteria B2:  “recurrent intrusions of elements of rapid eye movement (REM) sleep into the transition between sleep and wakefulness, as manifested by either hypnopompic or hypnagogic hallucinations or sleep paralysis at the beginning or end of sleep episodes”

Insert new criteria B2: “Hypocretin deficiency, as measured using cerebrospinal fluid hypocretin-1 immunoreactivity measurements (<1/3 of values obtained in healthy subjects tested during the same assay, or ≤110 pg/ml). Low cerebrospinal fluid hypocretin-1 must not be observed in the context of acute brain injury, inflammation or infection.”

Insert new criteria B3 “Nocturnal sleep polysomnography showing rapid eye movement (REM) sleep latency less than or equal to 15 minutes, or a Multiple Sleep Latency Test showing a mean sleep latency less than or equal to 8 minutes and two or more sleep onset REM periods.”

 

Delete Criteria C: The disturbance is not due to the direct physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug of abuse, a medication) or another general medical condition.”

Change #1.  Replace title of “narcolepsy” to “narcolepsy/hypocretin deficiency”

 

Reasons for the change:

The current diagnosis criteria are outdated and with time it became more evident that the only symptom that is specific for narcolepsy is cataplexy. Narcolepsy had been originally described with the presence of cataplexy[1, 2].  In the late 1950s however, it was discovered that patients with narcolepsy also have a short REM sleep latency, a finding that led to using the Multiple Sleep Latency Test (MSLT) as a diagnostic test for narcolepsy (mean sleep latency ≤ 5 minutes and more recently ≤8 minutes with ≥2 occurrence of REM sleep in 4-5 naps as a positive test). The MSLT is a good test, with few false positives and few false negatives (~95% sensitivity, ~95% specificity) [3].  Unfortunately, as first noted by Chervin et al. true narcolepsy cases have been submerged by a large number of patients who complain of daytime sleepiness without a clear cause, and were not found to have sleep apnea[4].  The high frequency of sleep apnea and polysomnography testing for this syndrome created a need to explain pathological sleepiness in patients without cataplexy and sleep apnea.  Studies in the general population have found that approximately 2-5% of subjects in the general population have a pathological MLST[5, 6], half of whom do not even complain of daytime sleepiness.

Overtime, the original definition of narcolepsy, which implicated presence of cataplexy in the majority of cases (typically 80% in early cases series) expanded to include anyone with a positive MLST suggesting abnormally short REM sleep latency, expanding the notion of narcolepsy.  Sleep paralysis and hypnagogic hallucinations, accessory REM sleep related symptoms found in patients with narcolepsy became as important as cataplexy as symptoms defining abnormal REM sleep, following publications by Yoss and Daly[7], although these can frequently be found in other sleep pathologies or even in normal individuals[8, 9].  This evolution occurred based on a clinical need and in the absence of scientific data.  The definition of narcolepsy was slowly “expanded” to include all patients with cataplexy (A plus original B1) and an increasing number of patients with unexplained sleepiness and either an abnormal MSLT (in the original International classification of sleep disorders, ICSD1) or hynagogic hallucinations/sleep paralysis (criteria B2 of the original DSMIV).

In 2000, it was discovered that most cases with narcolepsy and cataplexy have hypocretin deficiency[10-12]. Animal models without hypocretin have narcolepsy, establishing causality [13, 14].  More than 10 different groups from different countries across the world replicated this finding, demonstrating that almost all patients with cataplexy have low or undetectable levels of hypocretin-1 in the cerebrospinal fluid[15-23].  In contrast, only 5-30% (depending how carefully other causes of daytime sleepiness have been excluded) of cases without cataplexy were found to have low CSF hypocretin-1 (25-50% of cases with low CSF hypocretin and no cataplexy actually also develop cataplexy 1-40 years later after the beginning of sleepiness) [24]. 

This evolution was recognized by the ICSD2, second edition published in 2005 that separated narcolepsy with and without cataplexy as separate disease entities[25].  Further, as a larger number of patients became aware of narcolepsy and cataplexy, and as the condition is treated by potentially addictive medications (amphetamine like compounds) for life, it was felt that objective confirmation either by measuring CSF hypocretin or by having a positive MSLT was strongly advised (but not mandated).

The new DSMV name “narcolepsy/hypocretin deficiency” now encompasses a real disease entity with a single etiology and a consistent set of symptoms.  As it is revised, it is more similar to ICSD2 criteria for narcolepsy/cataplexy and will be almost identical to ICSD3 proposed criteria for a similar entity (to be called Type I narcolepsy, as they found the term hypocretin deficiency “clumsy”).  We prefer hypocretin deficiency as it is more descriptive and already extensively used in the literature.  Type 1 versus type 2 narcolepsy aims at mimicking the type 1 versus type 2 diabetes nomenclature, but in the case of diabetes mellitus, type 2 diabetes cases are insulin resistant, which is not known for hypocretin resistance in the proposed type 2 narcolepsy.

Therapy is more codified for this biologically defined entity, which was previously “contaminated” by 5-50% (depending of the case series) of patients with other problems of multiple etiologies, previously called narcolepsy without cataplexy and already separated in the ICSD2 as narcolepsy without cataplexy.

Evidence for the change: The proposed change is supported by strong scientific evidence (for details, see individual criteria changes):

  1. Narcolepsy with cataplexy is almost always caused by hypocretin deficiency.  Multiple studies across countries and ethnic groups have shown that 70-100% of cases with cataplexy have low CSF hypocretin-1 when this biochemical measure is assessed. In Mignot et al., [17] for example, data showed that “in cases with definite cataplexy, specificity (99%) and sensitivity (89%) for the CSF test was high”.  Similarly, Dauviliers et al., [21] found “low CSF hypocretin-1 is highly specific (99.1%) and sensitive (88.5%) for narcolepsy with cataplexy”.

  1. In contrast, 0-30% only of narcolepsy cases without cataplexy have normal CSF hypocretin-1, indicating that loss of hypocretin is not causally involved in producing symptoms in most of these cases.  Dauvilliers et al. [21] for example found no subjects without cataplexy and low CSF hypocretin, Knudsen et al. [23] found 21% and Mignot et al.[17] found 30%. 

  1. Symptoms such as sleep paralysis or hypnagogic hallucinations are frequent in the general population (4-6% for sleep paralysis, 40-6% for hypgnaogic hallucinations depending of how frequently these occur and how the questions are asked) [8, 9], are increased by sleep deprivation, and are also significantly associated with anxiety and depression in population based studies[26-28].   Although about half of narcoleptic patients with cataplexy [29]or hypocretin deficiency [17] have these symptoms and that those can be a problem clinically, they are not useful diagnostically having poor specificity and poor sensitivity.

The proposed change will increase etiological homogeneity for the condition based on new pathophysiological findings, the basis of all medical science.  All the findings above are undisputable and accepted by the scientific community and sleep clinicians. 

Integration with the International Classification of Sleep Disorders.  These revised criteria have already been mostly integrated in the current International Classification of Sleep Disorders 2 (ICSD2) second edition in 2005, which has been in use for ~ 7 years.  The ICSD2 classification already distinguishes narcolepsy with and without cataplexy as two separate disease entities, thus the DSMV is mostly catching up with established textbook nosology knowledge.  Of note, the new ICSD3 classification, the next iteration of the ICSD currently under development will also use very similar diagnostic criteria for a similar disease entity, although the name is still debated. The name may indeed be called type I narcolepsy (instead of narolepsy/hypocretin deficiency), in reference to type I diabetes due to the autoimmune destruction of insulin producing cells and thus insulin deficiency. Whether or not the name is the same, the two classifications will essentially cover the same entity, with minor details (the exact definition of what constitutes cataplexy for diagnostic purposes)

evidence for the change:  This is a simple clarification and an update. Cataplexy is both underdiagnosed and overdiagnosed thus the need to describe more exactly how it may manifest upfront in the criteria. Classically, cataplexy is indeed triggered by emotions but studies have shown the type of emotions is more important than the intensity [33, 34].  For example, fear, anxiety, or a sudden sound are not good triggers.  The most specific trigger that distinguish best true cataplexy in narcolepsy patients from other experiences reported by non narcoleptic subjects is joking thus it has been incorporated [33-35].  Laughter is also commonly involved, although it may be more culturally dependent as mirth is really what triggers cataplexy in the context of laughter[33-35].

In addition, cataplexy is almost always very brief and patients reporting feeling weak and having to rest for 30 minutes to several hours almost never have narcolepsy[33, 34]. Less than 15% of over 400 patients report that their cataplexy attacks usually last longer than 2 minutes[33]. Five-10% report having attacks 1 per year or less, less than 20% at least once per month.   For this reason, a few times per months when untreated was felt reasonable, while when treated it could become rare.

Finally, as knowledge of the disorder has increased, more and more patients, notably young children are diagnosed very close to a rapid and catastrophic onset that can occur in a few weeks.  In these cases, cataplexy manifest often differently, by semiconstant, tic like partial hypotonia of the jaw with tongue opening or even a generalized weakness that does not have any clear trigger [36-38].  These cases, rarely seen before, evolve into the more classic form (with usual trigger) 6 month to 1 year later. 

Thanks to improved treatment modalities (especially sodium oxybate), patients can now have months or even years without any cataplectic attacks.  For this reason, a caveat was added regarding low frequency in some successfully treated patients. 

The definition of cataplexy proposed here is fairly similar to that currently underwork in the ICSD3 (not surprisingly since they started with our definition). In the ICSD3, cataplexy is defined as more than one episode of generally brief (< 2 minutes), usually bilaterally   symmetric episodes of sudden loss of muscle tone with retained consciousness precipitated by strong emotions, usually positive, with almost always some episodes precipitated by emotions associated with laughter. The finding of transient reversible loss of deep tendon reflexes during an attack, while rare, is a strong diagnostic finding. In children (and rarely adults) cataplexy may present close to disease onset as facial (or generalized) hypotonia with droopy eyelids, mouth opening and protruded tongue or gait unsteadiness not clearly related to emotion. Facial and masticatory movements may occur. In children, anticipation of reward is a common precipitant. It is important to use child-appropriate contexts and language when trying to elicit a history of cataplexy in children.

In the DSM3 most of these details are in the text.

 

Change #4 Delete criteria B2:  “recurrent intrusions of elements of rapid eye movement (REM) sleep into the transition between sleep and wakefulness, as manifested by either hypnopompic or hypnagogic hallucinations or sleep paralysis at the beginning or end of sleep episodes”

Reasons and evidence for the change: Hypnopompic or hypnagogic hallucinations or sleep paralysis at the beginning or end of sleep episodes are neither sensitive nor specific for narcolepsy[8, 9], are increased by sleep deprivation, and are also significantly associated with anxiety and depression in population based studies[26-28].   Although about half of narcoleptic patients with cataplexy [29]or hypocretin deficiency [17] have these symptoms and that those can be a problem clinically, they are not useful diagnostically having poor specificity and poor sensitivity.

Evidence for change: The proposed change is supported by evidence based

 

Change #5. Insert new criteria B2: “Hypocretin deficiency, as measured using cerebrospinal fluid hypocretin-1 immunoreactivity measurements (<1/3 of values obtained in healthy subjects tested during the same assay, or ≤110 pg/ml). Low cerebrospinal fluid hypocretin-1 must not be observed in the context of acute brain injury, inflammation or infection.”

Reasons for the change:  The biological basis of the disorder has been established.  This criteria was already included in the ICSD2 as diagnostic for narcolepsy. It will also be in the ICSD3 as is.  The entire disorder is redefined based on pathophysiology and this particular biological test (see name change).  Even if not advised/necessary in clear cases, this test is helpful in clarifying diagnosis of some complex cases, for example subjects where cataplexy is of psychogenic origin, when there is confounding psychiatric or other comorbidities, or when sleep tests cannot be performed because the patient is taking medications that can change REM sleep occurrence (most psychotropic compounds) and cannot be easily stopped[39].

Evidence for the change: The finding of low CSF hypocretin-1 in narcolepsy has been replicated by more than ten laboratories across all continents except Africa Hong, 2002 #130}[16-23].  The particular threshold of 110 pg/ml or 1/3 of mean control values has been established by Quantitative Receiving Operating Curves in comparisons to healthy controls, cases with other sleep disorders (including narcolepsy without cataplexy, hypersomnia, insomnia), and samples drawn from patients with various acute or chronic neurological conditions [17].  As part of this extensive survey, it was found that normal subjects essentially never have low CSF hypocretin levels, but that some patients with severe acute neurological disorders (acute menigintis, severe head trauma, etc, generally comatose) can have decreased CSF hypocretin, a finding that is reversible when/if the condition improves. For this reason a caveat regarding interpretation of this test in severe acute neurological disorder was added [17, 24].

 

Change #6. Insert new criteria B3 “Nocturnal sleep polysomnography showing rapid eye movement (REM) sleep latency less than or equal to 15 minutes, or a Multiple Sleep Latency Test showing a mean sleep latency less than or equal to 8 minutes and two or more sleep onset REM periods.”

Reasons for the change: Past criteria were entirely subjective and did not correspond with clinical practice and insurance policy that typically mandates a positive sleep test as an objective finding, generally a Multiple Sleep Latency Test (MSLT) showing a mean sleep latency less than or equal to 8 minutes and two or more sleep onset REM periods.  This is important to avoid prescription of amphetamine like compounds broadly without objective evidence (sleep test or CSF hypocretin).  More recently, studies have shown that the observation of a short REM latency during a nocturnal sleep study is more specific (~99%) but less sensitive (~50%) than a positive MSLT (SE~95%, SP~ 95%) (unpublished data submitted to JAMA).  Based on this recent evidence, sleep test criteria consistent with narcolepsy can be either a SOREMP at night (rapid eye movement (REM) sleep latency less than or equal to 15 minutes) or a positive MSLT.  The advantage of this improved criteria that will also be incorporated in the revised ICSD3, is that if the sleep study performed the night before the MSLT shows a SOREMP (50%) of the cases, the daytime MSLT test can be cancelled, saving time and money without increasing false positive significantly.

Evidence for the change: Specificity and sensitivity for the MSLT for narcolepsy –cataplexy have all shown ~95% sensitivity and specificity either when cataplexy is used as a gold standard or when CSF hypocretin has been measured [3, 17, 40].  It is a good test to objectively confirm narcolepsy/hypocretin deficiency when cataplexy is present.  Rationale for the addition of the short REM latency data during a nocturnal sleep study is described in the publication submitted to JAMA.

 

Change #7. Delete Criteria C: The disturbance is not due to the direct physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug of abuse, a medication) or another general medical condition.”

Reason and evidence for the change:  Hypocretin deficiency and cataplexy cannot be caused by substance abuse, drugs, psychological conditions, or general medical or neurological conditions[17, 24].  A few rare syndromes, tumoral/surgical lesions of the hypothalamus are associated with genuine hypocretin cell loss and deficiency, but in these cases the appellation narcolepsy/hypocretin deficiency is still valid[17].  Further, at time, it is difficult to know if the association with another disorder such as a tumor is not a chance association between an autoimmune loss of hypocretin (the major cause of hypocretin deficiency) and another condition that is a mere precipitating factor, as alreasy pointed out before the discovery of hypocretin deficiency as the cause of narcolepsy [41].  The ICSD3 will also remove a similar statement contained in the ICSD2.

Narcolepsy

A. Irresistible attacks of refreshing sleep that occur daily over at least 3 months.

B. The presence of one or both of the following:

(1) cataplexy (i.e., brief episodes of sudden bilateral loss of muscle tone, most often in association with intense emotion)

(2) recurrent intrusions of elements of rapid eye movement (REM) sleep into the transition between sleep and wakefulness, as manifested by either hypnopompic or hypnagogic hallucinations or sleep paralysis at the beginning or end of sleep episodes

C. The disturbance is not due to the direct physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug of abuse, a medication) or another general medical condition.
