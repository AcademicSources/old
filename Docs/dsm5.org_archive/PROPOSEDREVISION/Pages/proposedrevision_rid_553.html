R 06 Caffeine Withdrawal

  • Proposed  Revision

Caffeine Withdrawal

  Updated April 30, 2012

A. Prolonged daily use of caffeine.

B. Abrupt cessation of caffeine use, or reduction in the amount of caffeine used, followed within 24 hours by three or more of the following symptoms:

1.      headache

2.      marked fatigue or drowsiness

3.      dysphoric mood, depressed mood, or irritability

4.      difficulty concentrating

5.      flu-like symptoms, nausea, vomiting, or muscle pain/stiffness

C. The symptoms in Criterion B cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

D. The symptoms are not associated with the direct physiological effects of another medical condition (e.g., migraine, viral illness) and are not better accounted for by another mental disorder.

Caffeine Withdrawal Disorder:

Proposal to (1) Move it from the Research Diagnostic section (DSM-IV) to the Substance Use Disorders section of DSM-5, and (2) Modify the criteria.

April 21, 2011

DSM-IV-TR and ICD-10 diagnostic criteria for caffeine withdrawal

The DSM-IV-TR included Caffeine withdrawal as a proposed diagnosis to encourage further research on the range and specificity of caffeine withdrawal symptoms (Hughes 1994). The major reason caffeine was not included in DSM versions was stated in DSM-III-R in 1990: “Although the existence of caffeine withdrawal is well-established, the syndrome is usually not severe enough to warrant clinical attention (p 138).” In contrast, ICD-10 includes a caffeine withdrawal disorder. This document provides our rationale for changing the criteria and moving Caffeine Withdrawal into the Substance Use section of the DSM-5.

Recent research on caffeine withdrawal

The great majority of the research literature on caffeine withdrawal has been published since the DSM-IV Work Group (in 1994) formulated its criteria for proposed research diagnoses and since ICD-10 was published (in 1992). Several review articles on caffeine as a drug of dependence have appeared (Ogawa and Ueki 2007; Griffiths and Ressig 2008). More specifically, prior articles have called for inclusion of caffeine withdrawal in DSM (Hughes et al. 1992; Strain and Griffiths 2005; Juliano and Griffiths, 2004; Juliano et al. 2009, 2011). In addition, Juliano and Griffiths (2004) have conducted a comprehensive review of the literature on caffeine withdrawal, using electronic search methods and secondary strategies to identify 57 experimental studies and 9 survey studies (see attached). Additional reports providing information on caffeine withdrawal have been published since that review (e.g., Satel 2006; Miyata et al. 2008; Roehrs and Roth 2008; Keane et al. 2007; Ozsungur et al. 2009; Sigmon and Griffiths, 2011).

Symptoms of caffeine withdrawal

In reviewing the experimental studies of caffeine withdrawal, Juliano and Griffiths (2004) used a conservative criteria for concluding that a given caffeine withdrawal symptom was valid: the statistical demonstration of the symptom in six or more studies that include two or more double-blind studies that used methodologies in which the conclusion of caffeine withdrawal effects was not confounded by the direct effects of caffeine. Of 49 symptom categories identified, the following 10 fulfilled validity criteria: headache, fatigue, decreased energy/activeness, decreased alertness, drowsiness, decreased contentedness, depressed mood, difficulty concentrating, irritability, and foggy/not clearheaded. In addition, flu-like symptoms, nausea/vomiting, and muscle pain/stiffness were judged likely to represent valid symptom categories.

Clinically significant distress or impairment

As stated above, the major reason caffeine withdrawal was not included in DSM-IV was lack of evidence of clinical significance. The DSM-IV-TR (2000) defines clinical significance as “present distress (e.g. a painful symptom) or disability (i.e., impairment in one or more areas of functioning) or with a significantly increased risk of suffering death, pain, disability or an important loss of freedom. (p xxxi)” Caffeine withdrawal can produce distress, impairment and loss of freedom.

Prospective experimental studies showing clinically significant distress or impairment

The severity of caffeine withdrawal symptoms can vary from mild to extreme. Juliano and Griffiths (2004) reviewed 14 published reports reporting clinically significant distress and/or impairment of normal daily activities (e.g., work absence or going to bed because of symptoms) upon caffeine abstinence. The median percentage of subjects reporting headache was about 50% in experimental studies and 24% in retrospective survey studies. However, of most importance to considerations of DSM-5, is the incidence of clinically significant distress or impairment in daily functioning. Such information is available from several prospective experimental studies and retrospective survey studies.

Seven prospective experimental studies provide information on the incidence of clinically significant distress or functional impairment during caffeine abstinence. With regard to the experimental studies, in one double-blind study of 11 people who fulfilled criteria for DSM-IV substance dependence applied to caffeine use (median intake 357 mg/day; i.e. about 1.3 times US average of caffeine consumers), 73% experienced significant disruptions in normal daily activities during a period of experimentally-induced caffeine abstinence, including leaving or missing work, making errors or costly mistakes at work, inability to care for children, and inability to complete school work (Strain et al. 1994). In another study, 44 pregnant women at their first prenatal visit were advised by their obstetrician to eliminate all caffeine for the duration of pregnancy (Svikis et al. 2007). At subsequent prenatal visits, almost all women reported that they attempted to eliminate or reduce caffeine use. More than half reported withdrawal symptoms and 14% of the total sample reported that the withdrawal symptoms were severe enough to interfere with their performance at work, school, or home.

Five experimental studies have been conducted in general subject populations. One study was conducted 60+ yrs ago in 22 medical and graduate students who consumed 650-780 mg/day (about 2.6x US average of caffeine consumers). When abstinence was tested, headache “as extreme in severity as the subjects had ever experienced” occurred in 55% of 38 trials (Driesbach and Pfeiffer 1943). A double-blind study of 62 caffeine consumers (mean intake 235 mg/day) who had no knowledge that the study was about caffeine, found that during caffeine abstinence 52% of subjects reported moderate to severe headache, and 8-11% showed abnormally high scores on standardized depression and fatigue scales (Silverman et al. 1992). In an open-ended interview, several of these subjects also reported severe functional impairment. In another double-blind study 45% of 40 caffeine consumers (mean intake 360 mg/day) reported a “diffuse, throbbing headache”, with 28% of those also reporting “nausea and sickness” (Lader et al. 1996). A third double-blind study evaluated the incidence of functional impairment during abrupt caffeine abstinence in a group of 18 subjects (mean intake 231 mg/day) who reported having had problems or symptoms when previously stopping caffeine (Dews et al. 1999). The study found that 39% of subjects spontaneously reported caffeine withdrawal symptoms and 22% showed substantial decreases (≥1.5 points on a 4 point scale) in their ratings of daily functioning (e.g., at work and leisure activities), although none of the subjects reported symptoms judged to be “incapacitating” by the authors. Finally, one non-blind trial of caffeine abstinence reported 10% of 20 caffeine consumers reported nausea and vomiting (Couturier et al. 1997).

Overall, the incidence of clinically significant or functional impairment in prospective experimental studies with normal subjects has varied from about 10% to as high as 55% (median 13%). These incidence rates could underestimate the rate of occurrence in the general population because individuals who experience clinically significant withdrawal may be less likely to volunteer for a study in which caffeine abstinence might be a possibility.

Retrospective survey studies showing clinically significant distress or impairment

Five retrospective studies provide information on the percentage of respondents reporting clinically significant distress or functional impairment during caffeine abstinence. A survey study done over 40 yrs ago of 183 women showed that the percentage endorsing that they would experience classic caffeine withdrawal symptoms (headache, lethargy and sleepiness) without their morning coffee increased as a function of the number of cups of coffee consumed (Goldstein and Kaizer 1969). Consistent with functional impairment, endorsement of being “unable to work effectively” also increased with cups consumed, with the effect reported by 9% of all coffee consumers and 16% of heavy consumers (>5 cups/day). In a population-based random digit dial telephone survey study, caffeine consumers were questioned about their experience with caffeine withdrawal (Hughes et al. 1998). Of 71 who reported having stopped or reduced caffeine for at least 24 hours during the past year, 11% reported experiencing headache and other withdrawal symptoms that interfered with their performance. This figure was 24% among the subgroup who reported stopping caffeine in an attempt at permanent abstinence. A third study asked a wide variety of questions of people who telephoned to volunteer for a paid clinical research trial (Dews et al. 1999). Of 6,815 daily caffeine consumers, 2.6% reported that problems or symptoms during caffeine abstinence were severe enough to interfere with normal activity (e.g., inability to concentrate at work; lost time at work). A retrospective survey was conducted in 36 adolescent caffeine consumers who endorsed two or more DSM-IV substance dependence criteria applied to caffeine use (Oberstar et al. 2002). Twenty-one percent reported the caffeine withdrawal symptom of “sick/nauseated/vomiting.” Finally, in a study by Richards et al. (2004), local advertising identified 74 individuals who were sufficiently distressed by their caffeine use to seek treatment to cut back or eliminate their caffeine consumption. In a clinical interview, 50% of the sample reported that the severity of previously experienced withdrawal symptoms had been sufficient to produce an impairment of normal activities (i.e. functional impairment). Examples of functional impairment included: calling in sick to work; mistakes or low productivity at work; irritability in co-worker interactions, and over-sleeping because of fatigue and lack of motivation.

Overall, the percentage of respondents reporting clinically significant distress or functional impairment in retrospective survey studies varied between 2.6% and 11% (median 9%) in general survey studies, was 21% in caffeine dependent adolescent subjects, and 50% in interviews with adults seeking treatment for caffeine use. The median estimate from the general survey studies is quite similar to the estimate from prospective experimental studies (9% and 13%, respectively). As with the experimental studies, there are several limitations inherent to survey studies that may result in underestimates. First, a portion of habitual caffeine consumers may be unaware of caffeine withdrawal symptoms because they never have had a period of sustained abstinence. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that as little as 25 mg/day of caffeine can prevent some withdrawal symptoms (Evans and Griffiths 1999). Thus, small amounts of caffeine that are unknowingly consumed on days believed to be “caffeine-free” days may lead to an underestimation of the incidence of clinically significant distress during complete abstinence. Finally, it is possible that many caffeine users are unaware that caffeine abstinence can cause withdrawal and, thus, caffeine withdrawal symptoms such as headache, fatigue nausea and muscle aches could be misattributed to other causes or ailments (e.g., common cold).

Impairment of Freedom

Several lines of evidence indicate repeated caffeine use can produce a physical dependence state that impairs the users freedom (i.e., impairs his/her ability to voluntary stop caffeine use). Research indicates that avoidance of abstinence-associated withdrawal symptoms plays a central role in the habitual consumption of caffeine. This relationship has been shown in retrospective questionnaire studies (Goldstein and Kaizer 1969) and in double-blind experimental studies that assessed direct behavioral measures of caffeine reinforcement or preference (Griffiths et al. 1986; Hughes et al. 1993; Liguori and Hughes 1997; Schuh and Griffiths 1997; Garrett and Griffiths 1998) and beverage flavor preferences (Rogers et al. 1995; Yeomans et al. 1998; Yeomans et al. 2000; Yeomans et al. 2001; Yeomans et al. 2002; Tinley et al. 2003). One study of caffeine reinforcement, for example, showed that moderate caffeine consumers who reported caffeine withdrawal symptoms (i.e., headache, drowsiness) after drinking decaffeinated coffee were more than twice as likely to choose caffeinated over decaffeinated coffee in choice tests (Hughes et al. 1993). In studies that prospectively manipulated caffeine physical dependence, subjects chose caffeine more than twice as often when they were physically dependent than when they were not physically dependent (Griffiths et al. 1986; Garrett and Griffiths 1998).

Several studies have characterized individuals who endorse the substance dependence criteria of having “persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down or control substance use” with regard to caffeine (Strain et al. 1994; Hughes et al. 1998; Oberstar et al. 2002; Richards et al. 2004; Svikis et al. 2005; Jones and Lejuez 2005; Striley and Cottler 2009). In a population-based study of caffeine users, among those who had tried to quit, only 37% were successful (Hughes and Oliveto, 1997). Studies show that individuals who have a persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down or control substance use also endorse high rates of using caffeine to avoid withdrawal (Strain et al. 1994; Oberstar et al. 2002; Richards et al. 2004; Jones and Lejuez 2005. For example, in the study by Richards et al. (2004), 89% of a group of individuals who were seeking treatment to reduce or eliminate their caffeine consumption endorsed using caffeine to relieve or avoid withdrawal. In two general population studies of caffeine use, 17% and 24% endorsed using caffeine to relieve or avoid withdrawal (Hughes et al. 1998, and Striley and Cottler 2009). Finally, a study by Svikis et al. (2005) investigated the inability to quit caffeine among a group of women who were given verbal and written instructions to quit caffeine during their pregnancy in order to avoid adverse birth consequences. Thirteen percent continued to use caffeine in amounts greater than those thought to be safe during pregnancy (>300 mg/day). Withdrawal symptoms featured prominently among examples of reasons women provided for failing to eliminate or cut back caffeine use: cravings/headache/nervous; migraines; need to stay awake/headaches; severe withdrawal; and couldn’t concentrate at work.

Finally, some have argued that because few caffeine users seek treatment, this indicates lack of clinical significance. We believe this lack of treatment seeking is not because caffeine withdrawal cannot cause significant distress, but is because a diagnosis must be recognized by the general public and health professionals, and there must be some awareness of the possibility of treatment before much treatment-seeking occurs.

 Psychometric Tests of the Validity of Caffeine Withdrawal.

Caffeine withdrawal has undergone several validity tests. First, as reviewed above, caffeine withdrawal has predictive and clinical validity (i.e., it prospectively predicts the ability to abstain) (Hughes et al. 1993). Several other tests of psychometric validity have been made. For example, test-retest reliability of the syndrome is high and withdrawal measures show construct validity; e.g. are related to caffeine intake (Hughes et al. 1998; Juliano and Griffiths 2004).

Prior studies have also examined the factor structure of caffeine withdrawal (Hughes et al. 1998; Evans and Griffiths 1999). In 2004, based on the their evaluation of the research literature, Juliano and Griffiths (2004) proposed five conceptual clusters (i.e. factors) of withdrawal symptoms, and they proposed that part B of the DSM research criteria be changed to indicate that a caffeine withdrawal diagnosis require: Abrupt cessation of caffeine use, or reduction in the amount of caffeine used, closely followed by three or more of the following: (1) headache, (2) fatigue or drowsiness, (3) dysphoric mood, depressed mood, or irritability, (4) difficulty concentrating, and (5) flu-like somatic symptoms, nausea, vomiting, or muscle pain/stiffness. It should be recognized that although the individual symptoms were empirically validated, the symptom clusters were conceptually rather than empirically derived. Juliano and Griffiths (2004) suggested that future research use statistical procedures to empirically differentiate among clusters of symptoms.

Two recent studies provide an empirical basis for clustering caffeine withdrawal symptoms. A retrospective study by Ozsungur et al. (2009) used a principal components factor analysis to identify common underlying factors among the major caffeine withdrawal symptoms previously described by Juliano and Griffiths. The analysis showed three clusters: 1. fatigue and headache, 2. dysphoric mood, and 3. flu-like somatic symptoms. Difficulty concentrating was weighted equally on factors 1 and 2. There were a number of limitations of the study including: 1. the subjects had a relatively low caffeine intake (mean 168 mg/day); 2. caffeine symptoms were rated retrospectively based on naturally-occurring periods of abstinence with no assurance that they were, in fact, entirely caffeine-free; 3. it is not clear if participants endorsed symptoms based on one discrete incident of caffeine withdrawal or based on different incidents, which is important to a meaningful analysis of clustering of symptoms in a discrete episode of caffeine withdrawal; 4. with regard to whether or not fatigue and headache are independent, Juliano and Griffiths cited five reports of involving case studies, correlation analysis, and cluster analysis of withdrawal symptoms that indicated that non-headache symptoms and signs of caffeine withdrawal do not always co-vary with the presence of headache, and can occur in the absence of headache. Thus, Juliano and Griffiths concluded, non-headache symptoms represent distinct features of the caffeine withdrawal syndrome that can occur independently of headache.

In a prospective study, Juliano et al. (in preparation) had 213 caffeine users (mean 549.95 mg per day) abstain from caffeine overnight and then evaluated their self-reported withdrawal symptoms using a 23-item withdrawal measure. A principle components analysis of the measure indicated a 7-factor solution that accounted for 68% of the variance. The factors were as follows: 1. drowsiness/fatigue, 2. decreased alertness/concentration, 3. mood disturbances, 4. decreased sociability/motivation to work, 5. nausea/upset stomach, 6. flu-like feelings, and 7. headache. The correlations among factors ranged from .21 to .54 indicating that although the factors shared some common variance they were not redundant. Withdrawal scores were higher during abstinence compared to a preceding baseline period involving ad libitum consumption on all factors except for nausea/upset stomach. Likewise, withdrawal scores were higher during abstinence compared to caffeine administration on all factors except for nausea/upset stomach. Although these results are preliminary and were based only on a period of overnight abstinence, the derived clusters were based on prospective data and were quite similar to those proposed by Juliano and Griffiths (2004). This factor analysis suggests an additional cluster relating to decreased sociability/motivation for work.

In summary, caffeine withdrawal appears be psychometrically sounds; e.g., it has prospective validity and a clear factor structure that can be used to inform diagnostic criteria for caffeine withdrawal.

 

Proposed revision of DSM-IV-TR diagnostic criteria for caffeine withdrawal

Most of the research literature on caffeine withdrawal has been published since the DSM-IV Work Group (in 1994) formulated its criteria for proposed research diagnoses. We believe that the research now clearly documents that caffeine withdrawal may be severe enough to warrant clinical attention, which was questioned in DSM-III-R (American Psychiatric Association 1987). Moreover, the research literature now provides a sound empirical basis for updating the criteria for caffeine withdrawal. Problems with the DSM-IV-TR criteria are that they: 1. do not reflect the apparent independence of headache and non-headache withdrawal symptoms (literature reviewed by Juliano and Griffiths 2004); 2. do not include symptoms that have now been well-validated; 3. include a symptom (i.e., anxiety) for which there is little empirical support. The decision to propose the diagnosis based on three of the five clusters was a conservative strategy to prevent over-diagnosis of the disorder, which was also a concern of the DSM-IV Work Group (Hughes 1994). However, based on a large body of current research, a greater number of clusters appears to be warranted.

More specifically, based on a substantial body of empirical research and several studies that have conceptually and empirically defined caffeine withdrawal symptom clusters, it is proposed that part B of the DSM research criteria be changed to indicate that a caffeine withdrawal diagnosis require: Abrupt cessation of caffeine use, or reduction in the amount of caffeine used, closely followed by three or more of the following: (1) headache, (2) marked fatigue or drowsiness, (3) dysphoric mood, depressed mood, or irritability, (4) difficulty concentrating, and (5) flu-like somatic symptoms, nausea, vomiting, or muscle pain/stiffness. Although the Juliano et al. factor analysis suggests an additional cluster of decreased sociability/motivation for work, this criterion appears to lack specificity and may produce a higher rate of diagnosis. Furthermore, if this factor is of sufficient severity, it would likely be represented by part C of the diagnosis, which states that the symptoms in criterion B cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

A limitation of the proposed diagnostic criteria is that proposed symptoms have high prevalence and other etiologies. However, this is also true of other withdrawal diagnoses recognized by DSM (e.g., cocaine withdrawal, nicotine withdrawal, opioid withdrawal). A recent prospective within-subjects study involving counterbalanced periods of abstinence from caffeine and nicotine found no differences in the psychosocial manifestations (e.g., well-being; social functioning) of abstinence for nicotine and caffeine (Miyata et al. 2008).

Based on the above, we recommend that Caffeine Withdrawal be added to DSM-5. Our specific recommendations for the criteria and text for the diagnosis are illustrated in Table 1.

Benefits of Inclusion of Caffeine Withdrawal

As reviewed above, many caffeine users may have caffeine withdrawal and misattribute it to other causes or ailments. If patients and their health care providers were more aware of caffeine withdrawal symptomatology, unnecessary health care utilization and costs could potentially be avoided. For example, caffeine withdrawal should be ruled out when patients present with headache and other typical caffeine withdrawal symptoms before administering expensive diagnostic tests or medications. For example, a simple 2 day caffeine abstinence test could assess for caffeine withdrawal headache and might eliminate the need for more expensive diagnostic procedures. In addition our anecdotal experience is that often psychiatrists ignore the possibility of caffeine withdrawal as a cause of headaches, fatigue, depression, etc. This may occur often when patients are admitted to caffeine-free inpatient units. The inclusion of caffeine withdrawal as a diagnosis in the DSM-5 will engender an awareness of this potentially clinically significant syndrome.

Appendix B Research Criteria for Caffeine Withdrawal

A.    Prolonged daily use of caffeine.

B.    Abrupt cessation of caffeine use, or reduction in the amount of caffeine used, closely followed by headache and one (or more) of the following symptoms:

1.    marked fatigue or drowsiness

2.    marked anxiety or depression

3.    nausea or vomiting

C.    The symptoms in Criterion B cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

D.    The symptoms are not due to the direct physiological effects of a general medical condition (e.g., migraine, viral illness) and are not better accounted for by another mental disorder.
