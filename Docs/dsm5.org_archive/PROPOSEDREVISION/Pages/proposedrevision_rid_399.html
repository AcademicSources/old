M 09 Rapid Eye Movement Sleep Behavior Disorder

  • Proposed  Revision
  • Rationale
  • Severity
  • DSM-IV

Rapid Eye Movement Sleep Behavior Disorder

Updated May 1, 2012

 

A.     Repeated episodes of arousal during sleep associated with vocalization and/or complex motor behaviors which may result in injury to the individual or bed partner.

B.     These behaviors arise during REM sleep and therefore usually occur greater than 90 minutes after sleep onset, are more frequent during the later portions of the sleep period, and uncommonly occur during daytime naps.

C.     Upon awakening, the individual is completely awake, alert, and not confused or disoriented.

D.     The observed vocalizations or motor behaviors often correlate with simultaneously occurring dream mentation leading to the report of “acting out” one’s dreams.

E.     The behaviors cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social or other important areas of functioning – particularly pertaining to distress to the bed partner or injury to self or the bed partner.

F.     At least one of the following is present:

1.      Sleep-related injurious, potentially injurious, or disruptive behaviors arising from sleep

2.      Abnormal REM sleep behaviors documented by polysomnographic recording

G.    REM sleep without atonia on polysomnographic recording ( the diagnosis can be made without polysomnographic documentation in the setting of a history suggestive of RBD in the  context of an established synucleinopathy diagnosis).

H.     The disturbance is not due to the direct physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug of abuse, a medication) or a general medical condition.

 

Rationale for Proposed Changes to Rapid Eye Movement Sleep Behavior Disorder 

Updated May 1, 2012

 

1. Reasons for the proposed change

 

This change will improve diagnostic specificity.

Predicted in animal experiments in the 1960s (Jouvet and Delorme, 1965), RBD was first described in humans 1986 (Schenck, et al., 1986). Since that time it has clearly been identified as one of the major types of parasomnias, likely second only in prevalence to the disorders of arousal (confusional arousals, sleepwalking, and sleep terrors). The clinical features, polysomnographic findings (present in nearly every case), and response to medication have been well-characterized (Schenck and Mahowald, 2002; Anderson and Shneerson, 2009; Aurora, et al., 2010; Montplaisir, et al., 2010). RBD is one of the most important causes of sleep-related injurious or violent behavior (Schenck, et al., 2009; Ohayon and Schenck, 2010).

The extraordinary relationship between RBD and neurodenerative disorders (particularly the synucleinopathies - Parkinson's disease, dementia with Lewy bodies, and multiple system atrophy) has now been clearly established. At least 50% of individuals with RBD presenting in sleep clinics will eventually (often with a delay of over 10 years) develop one of these conditions (Boeve, et al., 2007; Iranzo, et al., 2009; Claassen, et al., 2010; Schenck and Boeve, 2011). RBD may be more prevalent in psychiatric populations (Chiu and Wing, 1997; Ohayon, et al., 1997; Teman, et al., 2009). In addition, iatrogenic RBD induced by mediations commonly prescribed by psychiatrists including tricyclic antidepressants, SSRIs or SNRIs is becoming increasingly recognized (Winkelman and James, 2004; Hoque and Chesson Jr, 2010).

 

2. Primary evidence for the change

 

RBD in both humans and animal models is a well-established independent disorder which may result in dramatic and potentially violent or injurious behavior arising from REM sleep.

Underscoring the fact that RBD is clearly an independent disorder is: 1) the recent identification of a transgenic mouse model with deficient glycine and GABA transmission demonstrating the behavioral, motor, and sleep phenotypes of RBD (Brooks and Peever, 2011) and 2) documentation of important functional neuroimaging abnormalities in humans (Desseilles, et al., 2008; Iranzo, et al., 2010; Unger, et al., 2010).

The clinical phenotype has been well documented and biomarkers have been identified. The predictive validity of the relationship with neurodegenerative disorders is supported by the fact that the inclusion of RBD improves the diagnostic classification of dementia with Lewy bodies (Ferman, et al., 2011).
