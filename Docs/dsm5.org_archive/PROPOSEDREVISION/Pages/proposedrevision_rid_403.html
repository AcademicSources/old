Restless Legs Syndrome

Updated May 1, 2012 

 

A.     Each of the following criteria must be met. The patient reports:

1.     An urge to move the legs, usually accompanied or caused by uncomfortable and unpleasant sensations in the legs (for pediatric Restless Legs Syndrome the description of these symptoms should be in the child’s own words).

2.     The symptoms begin or worsen during periods of rest or inactivity.

3.     Symptoms are partially or totally relieved by movement.

4.     Symptoms are worse in the evening or at night than during the day or occur only in the night/evening.

 

B.    These symptoms are accompanied by significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, educational, academic, behavioral or other important areas of functioning indicated by the presence of at least one of the following:

1.     Clinical sleep disturbance (e.g., significant sleep onset or maintenance problems, nocturnal awakenings, non-restorative sleep)

2.     Fatigue or low energy

3.     Daytime sleepiness

4.     Cognitive impairments (e.g. attention,  concentration, memory, learning)

5.     Mood disturbance (e.g. irritability, dysphoria, anxiety)

6.     Behavioral Problems (e.g. hyperactivity, impulsivity, aggression)

7.     Impaired academic, educational or occupational function

8.     Impaired interpersonal/social functioning

9.     Negative impact on caregiver or family functioning (e.g., fatigue, sleepiness)

 

C.     Frequency: Remains under discussion

 

D.     Duration: Remains under discussion

 

E.     The occurrence of the above symptoms (Criteria A) are not solely accounted for by another medical or behavioral condition (e.g., positional discomfort, leg cramps, habitual foot tapping, arthritis, leg edema, and peripheral ischemia).  

 

F.     The sleep difficulty occurs despite adequate age-appropriate circumstances and opportunity for sleep.

Rationale for the Proposed Changed to Restless Legs Syndrome in DSM-5

Updated May 1, 2012

 

Summary:  The Sleep Disorders Workgroup recommends that Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS) be elevated to its own diagnostic category. The diagnosis of RLS is based primarily on patient self-report and history. RLS is a sensorimotor, neurologic sleep disorder characterized by a desire to move the legs or arms, usually associated with uncomfortable sensations typically described as creeping, crawling, tingling, burning, or itching. Symptoms are worse when the individual is at rest and frequent movements of the legs occur in an effort to relieve the uncomfortable sensations. Symptoms are worse in the evening or night and in some individuals occur only in the evening or night. Elevation of RLS to its own diagnostic category represents a substantial change from DSM IV in which there was a brief summary of RLS subsumed under the broader category of “Dyssomnia Not Otherwise Specified.” 

 

Below, we detail the evidence for each of these categories that support the elevation of RLS to its own diagnostic category. We also describe the proposed diagnostic criteria along with justifications, supporting evidence, and potential negative consequences.

 

____________________________

 

Change #1.  Elevation of Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS) to its own Diagnostic Category.

 

Reasons for the change: The rationale for elevation of Restless Legs Syndrome to its own diagnostic criteria was based upon strong empirical evidence of :

 

1) Significant prevalence of RLS in the general population.

2) Association of RLS with significant clinical and functional impairment.

3) Identified and replicated genetic markers for RLS.

4) Successful treatment response of RLS.

5) Evidence for a defined pathophysiological basis of RLS.

 

The evidence supporting each of these domains of support is detailed below.

The need for elevating Restless Legs Syndrome to its own diagnostic category is based upon these converging lines of evidence. The recent evidence supporting the genetic basis and pathophysiological pathways involved in RLS indicate that it is its own disorder with a defined phenotype. Further, moderate to severe forms of RLS are well-documented to be distinct from normal or behavioral variations, and are of clinical significance due to significant impairments in functioning and psychological distress.  The prevalence of RLS, its association with significant sleep disruption and documented increases in psychiatric symptoms and functional impairment, led the Sleep/Wake Workgroup to conclude that the diagnosis of RLS also meets the criteria for a mental disorder. The availability of effective treatments for alleviation of RLS further underscores the importance of providing for the diagnosis of this disorder in the DSM V.

 

1) Significant Prevalence of RLS in the General Population: Prevalence rates of RLS vary widely when broad criteria are utilized, but range from 2.7% to 7.2% when more defined criteria are employed. Allen et al. (2005) reported that 7.2% of their sample scored positively on the four core symptoms of the International Restless Legs Syndrome Study Group (IRLSSG) Rating Scale. The IRLSSG Rating Scale is the most widely used rating scale for assessing the severity of RLS (Walters et al., 2003). When frequency of symptoms was set to at least twice a week with moderate or severe distress, the prevalence rate is 2.7%, while when frequency of symptoms is set at a minimum of once per week, the most consistent prevalence rate noted is 4.5%.  Females are 1.5-2 times more likely than males to have RLS (Ohayon, O’Hara R, & Vitiello, 2011, Table 1, Page 3). 

 

Ohayon MM, O'Hara R, Vitiello MV. Epidemiology of restless legs syndrome: A synthesis of the literature. Sleep Medicine Review. 2011, Jul 25. [Epub ahead of print]. PMID: 21795081.

Walters AS, LeBrocq C, Dhar A, Hening W, Rosen R, Allen RP, Trenkwalder C, International Restless Legs Syndrome Study Group. Validation of the International Restless Legs Syndrome Study Group rating scale for restless legs syndrome. Sleep Medicine 2003; 4: 121-132.

 

2) Association with Significant Clinical and Functional Impairment: A key feature of RLS that resulted in the Sleep/Wake Workgroup’s proposal for its elevation to its own diagnostic category is that presence of RLS is documented to be associated with significant clinical and functional impairment.  RLS is well documented associated with reduced sleep time, sleep fragmentation and reports of more sleep disturbance. Objective PSG studies demonstrate significant, objective sleep abnormalities in RLS, with increased latency to sleep and higher arousal index as the most consistent findings (Allen et al., 2003, Page 107; Winkelman et al., 2009, Table 2, Page 775). 

 

What is clear is that forms of RLS severe enough to significantly impair functioning, or are associated with psychiatric disorders including depression and anxiety are conservatively estimated at 2-3% of the population (Hening et al., 2004, Page 240, and Table 5 243; Winkelmann, 2005, Table 1 and 2, Page 69; Cho et al., 2009, Table 4 and 5, Page 1072).  Individuals with RLS are at least twice as likely as control subjects to have symptoms of depression or anxiety. Recent controlled studies, using validated assessments, have shown significantly increased odds ratios for moderately or highly elevated depressive symptoms, major depression, generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder and posttraumatic stress disorder in those with RLS (Winkelmann et al., 2005, Table 1 and 2, Page 69; Cho et al., 2009, Table 4 and 5, Page 1072).  Significant impairment of health-related quality of life (HRQoL) has been found in RLS and remains after controlling for age, gender, and disease comorbidity (Allen et al., 2010, Page 33-35, Figures 2 and 3; Winkelman et al., 2009, Table 3, Page 776). Overall, RLS accounts for a major disease burden (Trotti & Rye, 2011, Page 662).

 

Allen RP, Stillman P, Myers AJ. Physician-diagnosed restless legs syndrome in a large sample of primary medical care patients in western Europe: Prevalence and characteristics. Sleep Medicine 2010;11:31-7.

Cho SJ, Hong JP, Hahm BJ, et al. Restless legs syndrome in a community sample of Korean adults: prevalence, impact on quality of life, and association with DSM-IV psychiatric disorders. Sleep 2009; 32:1069-76.

Hening W, Walters AS, Allen RP, Montplaisir J, Myers A, Ferini-Strambi L. Impact, diagnosis and treatment of restless legs syndrome (RLS) in a primary care population: the REST (RLS epidemiology, symptoms, and treatment) primary care study. Sleep Medicine 2004; 5(3):237-46.

Trotti LM, Rye DB. Restless legs syndrome. Handbook of Clinical Neurology, 2011; 100:661-73.

Winkelmann J, Prager M, Lieb R, et al. "Anxietas Tibiarum" Depression and anxiety disorders in patients with restless legs syndrome. Journal of Neurology, 2005; 252:67-71.

Winkelman JW, Redline S, Baldwin CM, Resnick HE, Newman AB, Gottlieb DJ. Polysomnographic and health-related quality of life correlates of restless legs syndrome in the Sleep Heart Health Study. Sleep 2009; 32(6):772-8.

 

 

3) Identified and Replicated Genetic Markers for RLS: Genome wide association studies (GWAS) have independently identified several common single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) to be associated with RLS. To date, RLS has been significantly associated with common genetic variants in intronic or intergenic regions in MEIS1, BTBD9, and MAP2K5 on chromosomes 2p, 6p and 15q respectively (Winkelmann et al., 2007, Page 1001-1002 and 2011, Table 1, Page 4). The association of these three variants has been independently replicated (Stefansson et al., 2007; Figure 1, Page 642; Winkelmann et al., 2011, Table 1, Page 4; Vilarino-Guell et al., 2008; Table 1, Page 426).  BTBD9 (on chromosome 6p) confers a very large (80%) excessive risk when even a single allele is present; because of the high frequency of this variant in individuals of European descent, the population attributable risk (PAR) approximates 50%.  Meis1 (chromosome 2p) and MAP2K5 (chromosome 15q) have been replicated across populations and when combined with BTBD9, account for 70% of the PAR for RLS. 

 

Stefansson H, Rye DB, Hicks A et al. A genetic risk factor for periodic limb movements in sleep. New England Journal of Medicine 2007; 357: 639–647.

Vilarino-Guell C, Farrer MJ, Lin SC. A genetic risk factor for periodic limb movements in sleep. New England Journal of Medicine 2008; 358: 425–427.

Winkelmann J, Schormair B, Lichtner P et al. Genome-wide association study of restless legs syndrome identifies common variants in three genomic regions. Nature Genetics 2007; 39: 1000-6.

Winkelmann J, Czamara D, Schormair B et al. Genome-wide association study identifies novel restless legs syndrome susceptibility loci on 2p14 and 16q12.1. PLoS Genetics 2011; 7(7):

 

4) Successful Treatment Response of RLS: RLS has been successfully treated with dopaminergics (primarily D2 and/or D3 non-ergot agonists) the most commonly used class with an FDA indication for RLS. Recent meta-analyses, randomized, placebo-controlled clinical trials with both ropinirole and pramipexole demonstrate efficacy. These trials provide significant evidence for pathophysiological substrates for RLS grounded in dysfunctional central dopaminergic pathways (Zintzaras et al., 2010, Table 1, Page 226-227; Figure 2-5, Page 229-233; Bliwise et al., 2005, Figure 3, Page 145). Medications with potential GABA modulation, such as pregabalin, gabapentin or other related compounds, may also be effective and Gabapentin enacarbil, an anticonvulsant, has also recently been FDA approved for the treatment of RLS (Trotti & Rye, 2011, Page 667-668, for review).

 

Bliwise DL, Freeman A, Ingram CD, Rye DB, Chakravorty S, Watts RL. Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, short-term trial of ropinirole in restless legs syndrome. Sleep Medicine 2005; 6:141-147. 

Trotti LM, Rye DB. Restless legs syndrome. Handbook of Clinical Neurology, 2011; 100:661-73.

Zintzaras E, Kitsios GD, Afroditi AP, Konitsiotis S, Miligkos M, Rodopoulou P, & Hadjigeorgiou GM. Randomized trials of dopamine agonists in restless legs syndrome: A systematic review, quality assessment, and meta-analysis. Clinical Therapeutics 2010; 32: 221-237.    

 

5) Evidence for Defined Pathophysiological Basis of RLS: Although the etiology of RLS is still being examined there is consistent evidence to support the involvement of disturbances in the central dopaminergic system in the pathophysiology of RLS. As mentioned above, RLS has been successfully treated with dopaminergics (primarily D2 and/or D3 non-ergot agonists) the most commonly used class with an FDA indication for RLS.  Iron metabolism has been determined to play a key role in the pathophysiology of RLS.  This may also be mediated through the need for brain iron in dopamine production and regulation of dopamine synaptic density, as well as in myelin synthesis and mitochondrial energy production. Mild iron deficiency, characterized by serum ferritin below 50 µg/L, has been associated with increased severity of RLS and repletion of iron stores from below 50-70 µg/L has diminished RLS symptoms (see Trotti & Rye, 2011, Page 665-666, for review).  While genetic markers, BTBD9 and Meis1, associated with RLS, have been implicated in embryonic limb formation, the MAP2K5 gene, is a mitogen-activated protein kinases involved in muscle cell differentiation and neuroprotection of dopamine neurons (Winkelmann et al., 2007, Page 663).

 

Trotti LM, Rye DB. Restless legs syndrome. Handbook of Clinical Neurology, 2011; 100:661-73.

Winkelmann J, Schormair B, Lichtner P et al. Genome-wide association study of restless legs syndrome identifies common variants in three genomic regions. Nature Genetics 2007; 39: 1000-6.

 

Change #2.  Choice of Criteria for Diagnosis of Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS).

 

In DSM IV, a description of the symptoms of RLS and additional features of this disorder was subsumed within the category Dyssomnias Not Otherwise Specified. Elevation of RLS to its own diagnostic category required identification of a systematic set of diagnostic criteria. With respect the proposed DSM V criteria for diagnosis of RLS, diagnostic Criteria A are in line with the essential diagnostic features as defined in the DSM IV, by 2002 NIH Workshop Development Conference, the International RLS study group revised criteria (Allen et al., 2003) and the International Classification of Sleep Disorders. We underscore that these criteria have been systematically utilized for the diagnosis of RLS as defined in the significant majority of the supporting literature, including i) epidemiological studies of prevalence of RLS; ii) the recent GWAS studies identifying genetic markers for RLS; iii) the investigations of the clinical impact of RLS; and iv) clinical trial treatment studies of RLS. 

 

The DSM V criteria add a clarification that symptoms of RLS in pediatric samples must be in the child’s own words.  Criteria B require that the symptoms of RLS are accompanied by significant distress or impairment in clinical, affective, social, occupational, educational, academic, behavioral or other important areas of functioning.  Criteria F require that the occurrence of the diagnostic symptoms of RLS are not solely accounted for by another medical or behavioral condition, including positional discomfort, leg cramps, habitual foot tapping, arthritis, leg edema, and peripheral ischemia.  Criteria E is common to all DSM V sleep disorders and requires that the sleep difficulty occurs despite adequate age-appropriate circumstances and opportunity for sleep.  The evidence to support specific criteria for frequency and duration of symptoms await the completion of secondary data analysis due to be completed by end of February.

 

Allen RP, Picchietti D, Hening WA, Trenkwalder C, Walters AS, Montplaisir J. Restless legs syndrome: diagnostic criteria, special considerations, and epidemiology. A report from the restless legs syndrome diagnosis and epidemiology workshop at the National Institutes of Health. Sleep Medicine 2003; 4:101-19.

The work group has not yet proposed severity for this disorder. Please continue to check this Web site for updates, as this will be forthcoming.

 

This new disorder is not listed in DSM-IV and was previously subsumed under Dysomnia Not Otherwise Specified.
