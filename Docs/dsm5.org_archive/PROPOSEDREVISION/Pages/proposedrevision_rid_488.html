A 04 Specific Language Impairment

  • Proposed  Revision
  • Rationale
  • Severity
  • DSM-IV

Updated December 9, 2010

A.    Specific Language Impairment (SLI) is a form of Language Impairment that is diagnosed when language abilities are below age expectations but non-linguistic developmental abilities are within age expectations. The child's first words are likely to be delayed, early grammatical and morphosyntactic forms may be late to appear, vocabulary size can be smaller and less varied than expected, awareness of the phonemic and morphemic structure of words can be delayed well into the school-age years, and utterances can be shorter and less complex than expected. Language performance must be below age expectations as measured by an individualized, standardized, culturally and linguistically appropriate psychometric measure. Nonverbal cognitive performance is not impaired.

B.    The low language abilities result in functional limitations in effective communication, social participation, academic achievement, or occupational performance, alone or in any combination.

C.    Criteria are not met for Intellectual Disability.

D.    Symptoms must be present in early childhood (but may not become fully manifest until language demands exceed limited capacities)

Updated December 9, 2010

New Diagnostic Classification of Specific Language Impairment

Specific Language Impairment (SLI) is defined as Language Impairment in which language abilities are below age expectations when other non-language developmental indicators are within or above age expectations. It is a clinical condition with a history of investigation dating back to the first half of the nineteenth century (Leonard, 1998). Approximately 7% of 5-6-year-old children meet criteria for SLI (Tomblin, et al., 1997). Current identification practices involve inclusionary and exclusionary criteria. The inclusionary criterion requires language abilities below age expectations and nonverbal cognitive abilities at or above age expectations. Classic definitions benchmark nonverbal IQ levels as 85 or higher, and can be extended down to include nonverbal IQ levels of 70-85. The exclusionary criteria rule out related disabilities such as hearing loss, Autism Spectrum Disorder, or Intellectual Disability. Conventional cut-offs for Language Impairment are in the range of 1 to 1.25 standard deviations below the population mean on language assessments. The analysis of Tomblin, Records, and Zhang, (1996) reported that a criterion of -1.25 standard deviations across 5 measures of language yielded an estimate of -1.14 standard deviations on a single composite score, which corresponds well to prevalent clinical practices that use -1 standard deviation as a referral level. Longitudinal studies document that 5-year-old children identified with a -1 SD criterion are likely to remain in that range at adolescence or beyond (Johnson, et al., 1999; Law, Tomblin, & Zhang, 2008; Rice, Hoffman, & Wexler, 2009; Rice, Wexler, & Hershberger, 1998). Genetic studies document that SLI is heritable. A candidate gene, KIAA0319 on chromosome 6, has been identified as having shared contributions to SLI and reading disability (Rice, Smith, & Gayan, 2009) in families ascertained by proband who meet the -1 standard deviation criterion. Other studies have identified quantitative trait loci for SLI on chromosomes 16 and 19 (SLIConsortium, 2002; SLIConsortium, 2004; Falcaro et al., 2008).

If the nonverbal intelligence criterion for SLI is expanded to include children with nonverbal IQ levels in the range of 70-85, the estimated prevalence is increased by 3%, to 10% of 5-6-year-old children (Tomblin et al., 1997). Performance on general language assessments suggests that children in the lower range of nonverbal IQ show the same overall profile of Language Impairment as children in the range of 85 and above (Tomblin & Zhang, 1999) so that the problem seems to be one of dimension more than type when broad measures are used. More detailed analyses of grammatical tense marking, however, suggests qualitative differences, such that children in the lower range persist in certain errors for longer periods than children with SLI in the 85+ nonverbal IQ range. Some genetics studies have included children across the full range as their working definition of SLI (SLI Consortium, 2002; SLI Consortium, 2004; Falcaro, et al., 2008). Clinical practice can include children in the range of 70+ nonverbal IQ as “SLI.”
