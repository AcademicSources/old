Major Somnolence Disorder (Hypersomnia Not Elswhere Classified)

Updated May 1, 2012

 

This disorder is the main specific disorder. It is attributed when all other subtypes of Hypersomnolence Disorders have been eliminated. All other sleep disorders responsible for excessive sleepiness must have been eliminated.

Rationale for the Proposed Changes to the Hypersomnolence Disorders

Updated May 1, 2012

Summary: The Sleep Disorders Workgroup recommends the following changes to the Hypersomnolence Disorder diagnostic criteria for consideration in DSM-5:

  1. Replacing the term “Hypersomnia” with “Hypersomnolence.
  2. Replacing “Primary Hypersomnia” and “Hypersomnia related to another mental/medical disorder” with Major Somnolence Disorder, with specification of clinically comorbid conditions
  3. Criterion A: Increasing the precision in the definition of excessive sleepiness symptoms
  4. Criterion A: Adding sleep inertia as a symptom
  5. Criterion B: Adding a minimum frequency criterion (i.e., 3 days per week) with excessive sleepiness
  6. Criterion B: Raising the minimum duration threshold from 1 to 3 months for excessive sleepiness.
  7. Division of Hypersomnolence disorders into 4 subtypes

Change #1. Replace the term “Hypersomnia” with “Hypersomnolence”.

Reasons for the change: The disorder is characterized by a complaint of excessive sleepiness, which can be expressed in two main categories of symptoms: 1) excessive quantity of sleep: extended nocturnal sleep or involuntary daytime sleep, and (2) deteriorated quality of wakefulness: Sleep propensity during wakefulness as shown by difficulty awakening or inability to remain awake when required. The term “hypersomnia” is therefore better suited to describe an excessive amount of sleep when “Hypersomnolence” refers to the main symptom excessive sleepiness. Recent progresses have shown that several individuals have excessive sleepiness, not explained by any other sleep disorder, but with normal amount of sleep. We propose to adjust the terminology to reflect this reality.

Evidence for the change: Many individuals with severe excessive sleepiness symptoms remained undiagnosed because the excessive sleepiness is related to the quality of wakefulness, not the quantity of sleep. This change will also contribute to harmonize criteria with the International Classification of Sleep Disorders (ICSD-II), which identified two types of idiopathic hypersomnia: “with long sleep time” and “without long sleep time” and the upcoming ICSD-3, which will remove the distinction between with and without long sleep time due to the lack of clinical distinction between these two disorders other than the sleep duration. The use of the term “Hypersomnolence” for the section on the disorders of excessive sleepiness will also contribute to harmonize the terminology between the DSM-5 and the ICSD-3, which proposes to replace Hypersomnia with Hypersomnolence as the header for their section on the disorders of excessive sleepiness.

Change #2. Replace the diagnoses of “Primary Hypersomnia” and “Hypersomnia related to another (psychiatric, medical) disorder” with “Major Somnolence Disorder”.

Reasons for the change: The proposed change in the terminology removes the need to make causal attributions between co-existing disorders. It also acknowledges the bi-directional or interactive effects between sleep disorders and co-existing medical and/or psychiatric disorders. Another reason for this change is that the construct of “secondary hypersomnia” may lead to under treatment; this new conceptualization underscores that the patient has a sleep disorder in its own right that warrants independent clinical attention. For these reasons, we recommend using “Major Somnolence Disorder.” The maximum specificity is reached and is “major somnolence” with two thresholds (≥ 3 days per week for a period of three months) and after having eliminated the concurrent diagnoses. The presence of associated disorders should be listed separately under comorbid conditions, but without such terms as “related to, due to, or associated with”, which often cannot be established. “Major Somnolence Disorder” shall be used only when all possible sleep-related diagnoses and Hypersomnolence sub-types have been eliminated (Figure 1) conferring its specificity.

Evidence for the change: This proposed amendment is supported by epidemiological and clinical data indicating that hypersomnolence often presents with another psychiatric or medical disorder (Ohayon et al, 2012; Dauvilliers et al., 2010). Adopting this new terminology would simplify differential diagnosis and eliminate the need for criteria D and E from DSM-IV-TR.

Change #3: Increasing the precision in the definition of excessive sleepiness symptoms

Reasons for the change: The clinical description of excessive sleepiness symptoms, “complaint of excessive sleepiness as evidenced by either prolonged sleep episodes or daytime sleep episodes that occur almost daily” was vague and unclear. Many individuals with severe excessive sleepiness symptoms remained undiagnosed because the excessive sleepiness is related to the quality of wakefulness, not the quantity of sleep. This change will also contribute to harmonize criteria with the International Classification of Sleep Disorders (ICSD-II), which identified two types of idiopathic hypersomnia: “with long sleep time” and “without long sleep time”.

In the DSM-IV-TR, no cut-point was given to delineate what was a prolonged sleep episode. The proposed cut-point of nine hours as indicative of a prolonged main sleep episode will help identify individuals with excessive amount of sleep. The choice of nine hours was based on 1) clinical evidence that individuals with hypersomnolence disorder (with or without long sleep time) sleep on average 8 to 8.5 hours on weekdays and 2) it represents the upper fifth percentile of the normal sleep distribution in the general population (Ohayon et al., 2012; table 2, p. 74).

The former symptom “daytime sleep episodes” was broken down in two symptoms: “recurrent naps within the same day” and “recurrent periods of irrepressible need to sleep or lapses into sleep within the same day”. This change will allow the inclusion of individuals who have a normal duration of the main sleep period, defined as at least 7 hours of sleep, and have recurrent sleepiness episodes within the same day (at least 2 episodes). A duration of 7 hours was adopted instead of the 6 hours proposed by the ICSD-II because it is the average sleep duration of healthy adults (Ohayon et al., 2004) but also to reduce the possibility of including individuals whose excessive sleepiness might be brought by insufficient sleep.

Evidence for the change: Excessive sleepiness symptoms, as described in the proposed changes, are highly prevalent in the general population (15.2%) (Ohayon et al., 2012, figure p. 75). The prevalence dropped to 4.7% when criterion B (frequency, duration and minimum main sleep period of 7 hours) was added and decreased again to 2.6% when criterion C (impairment/distress) was added. The prevalence of Major Somnolence Disorder was 1.5%: 0.5% with a long sleep time and 1.0% with deteriorated quality of wakefulness (Ohayon et al., 2012).

In individuals Hypersomnolence characterized by a prolonged main sleep period, as many as 81% reported the sleep being unrefreshing (Vernet et al., 2010).


Change #4: Addition of sleep inertia as a symptom

Reasons for the change: The addition of sleep inertia would increase the precision in the identification of hypersomnolent individuals. The symptom is present in about 50% of idiopathic hypersomnia with or without long sleep time (Vernet et al., 2010; Anderson et al., 2007; Bassetti and Aldrich, 1997). The association with excessive sleepiness symptoms is highly specific to hypersomnia. This change will also contribute to harmonize criteria with the upcoming ICSD3, in which, sleep inertia (sleep drunkenness) and unrefreshing naps have been proposed as supportive additional clinical features.

Evidence for the change: Difficulty in awakening from sleep is present in the majority of individuals (up to 78%) with hypersomnolence characterized by a long sleep time (Vernet et al., 2010, table 1 p.4). Sleep inertia (sleep drunkenness) has been reported in 21% to 72% of individuals with a hypersomnolence disorder (Anderson et al., 2007 (table 2 p.1277); Bassetti and Aldrich, 1997; Billiard and Dauvilliers, 2001; Vernet et al., 2009; 2010).

Change #5: Addition of a minimum frequency criterion (i.e., at least 3 days per week) with excessive sleepiness

Reasons for the change: This replaces the “almost daily” occurrence in the DSM-IV criterion A. The propose cut-point will help distinguishing individuals with occasional sleepiness from those with more severe symptoms of excessive sleepiness.

Evidence for the change: The frequency criteria (at least 3 times /week) was selected based on Ohayon et al (2012, table 2, p. 74) population survey, which indicated it was the best cut off for sleepiness in terms of identifying individuals reporting impairment/distress associated with excessive sleepiness.

Change #6. Raising the minimal duration threshold from 1 to 3 months for excessive sleepiness.

Reasons for the change: One month is a very short period to define excessive sleepiness as a chronic condition. This change will also contribute to harmonize criteria with the International Classification of Sleep Disorders (ICSD), which set the minimal duration for Hypersomnolence disorder to three months.

Evidence for the change: Hypersomnolence disorder is mostly a chronic condition, which typically begins in early adulthood (Ali et al., 2009; Anderson et al., 2007 (table 4 p. 1277); Bassetti and Alderich, 1997; Ozaki et al., 2008; Vernet et al., 2009; 2010).

Change #7. Division of Hypersomnolence disorders into 4 subtypes

Reasons for the change: In the past 20 years, the progresses in research on the disorders of excessive sleepiness have been considerable. The most remarkable advance has been with Narcolepsy. There is now sufficient scientific evidence to distinguish two broad subtypes: “Narcolepsy/hypocretin deficiency” and “Narcolepsy without Cataplexy” which both have specific subset of criteria for the diagnosis.

A third subtype, Periodic Hypersomnolence, Kleine-Levin Subtype, has received some scientific substantiation in the recent years and is clearly distinct from other forms of Hypersomnolence. The two largest studies conducted on the Kleine-Levin Subtype have shown consistent patterns of symptoms of cognitive abnormalities, memory defects, megaphagia and/or hypersexuality accompanied with excessive amount of sleep (Arnulf et al., 2005; Arnulf et al., 2008, table 2, p. 485).

Major Somnolence Disorder, is the main Hypersomnolence Disorder diagnosis after the differential diagnosis is applied.

Evidence for the change: Scientific evidence for “Narcolepsy/hypocretin deficiency” has been presented in that section. “Narcolepsy without Cataplexy” is characterized by excessive sleepiness accompanied by a mean sleep latency of less than 8 minutes and 2 or more Sleep Onset REM Periods (SOREMPs) during the Multiple Sleep Latency Test (MSLT) (Ozaki et al., 2008; Sasai et al., 2009; Takei et al., 2012).
