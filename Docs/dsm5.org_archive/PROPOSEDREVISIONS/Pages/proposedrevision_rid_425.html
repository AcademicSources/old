Manic Episode

  • Proposed  Revision
  • Rationale
  • Severity
  • DSM-IV

Updated April-28-2012

Manic Episode

 

Note: this is not a codeable disorder.

 

A.   A distinct period of abnormally and persistently elevated, expansive, or irritable mood and abnormally and persistently increased activity or energy, lasting at least 1 week and present most of the day, nearly every day (or any duration if hospitalization is necessary).

 

B.   During the period of mood disturbance and increased energy or activity, three (or more) of the following symptoms (four if the mood is only irritable) are present to a significant degree, and represent a noticeable change from usual behavior:

1.  Inflated self-esteem or grandiosity

2.  Decreased need for sleep (e.g., feels rested after only 3 hours of sleep)

3.  More talkative than usual or pressure to keep talking

4.  Flight of ideas or subjective experience that thoughts are racing

5.  Distractibility (i.e., attention too easily drawn to unimportant or irrelevant external stimuli), as reported or observed

6.  Increase in goal-directed activity (either socially, at work or school, or sexually) or psychomotor agitation (i.e., purposeless non-goal directed activity)

7.  Excessive involvement in activities that have a high potential for painful consequences (e.g., engaging in unrestrained buying sprees, sexual indiscretions, or foolish business investments)

 

C.   The mood disturbance is sufficiently severe to cause marked impairment in social or occupational functioning or to necessitate hospitalization to prevent harm to self or others, or there are psychotic features.

 

D.   The episode is not attributable to the direct physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug of abuse, a medication, or other treatment). Note: A full Manic Episode emerging during antidepressant treatment (medication, electroconvulsive therapy, etc), but persisting beyond the physiological effect of that treatment is sufficient evidence for a Manic Episode and, therefore, a Bipolar I diagnosis. However, caution is indicated so that one or two symptoms (particularly increased irritability, edginess or agitation following antidepressant use) are not taken as sufficient for diagnosis of a Manic Episode, nor necissarily an indication of a Bipolar Disorder diathesis.

 

Specify with Mixed Features.

 

Rationale

 

Change 1: The work group is proposing recognition of increased energy/activity as a core symptom of hypomania/mania

Mild mania, hypomania and subthreshold bipolar features are often unrecognized or misdiagnosed as major depressive disorder (MDD). The consequence of an unrecognized or missed diagnosis is the delay in the recognition of illness and the start of appropriate treatment, thus putting individuals at risk for a worsening course of illness, psychosocial impairment, non-response to treatment, and suicidal behavior.   Conversely, mild positive mood changes that are unrelated to bipolar disorder may be misdiagnosed as hypomania when the level of activity is not taken into account.  Central to the problem is the fact that the clinician is often seeing the patient in the depressive state and attempting to assess hypo/mania retrospectively.  In that situation, patients  may be more able to recall periods of increased activity than changes in mood that may be more subtle and ego-syntonic at the time (Benazzi 2003).

It is estimated that up to 31% of patients with major depression are erroneously diagnosed as having MDD instead of BD (Hirschfeld, Calabrese et al. 2003), with an average 7 to 10 year delay of correct diagnosis (Kupfer, Frank et al. 2002; Hirschfeld, Lewis et al. 2003). Individuals misdiagnosed as having MDD instead of Bipolar Disorder (BD) often receive inappropriate treatments for many years, in particular, antidepressant pharmacotherapy without mood stabilizer treatment, thus exposing them to the risk of cycle acceleration or hypomanic/manic switches, mixed states and suicidal behaviors. Improving diagnostic precision would be expected to increase recognition of bipolarity among individuals presenting with major depression and thus increase the probability of receiving early appropriate treatment and, thus, saving individuals years of unnecessary suffering and, in younger individuals, preventing the loss of critical developmental gains (Ghaemi, Boiman et al. 2000; Goodwin and Jamisson 2007).

Several investigators over the years have explored alternate criteria in order to improve diagnostic precision. One such modification is that being proposed, i.e. including increased activity/energy as a stem criterion for hypomanic and manic episodes.   It is currently possible to meet DSM-IV criteria for hypo/mania, and thus bipolar I or II disorder, without a change in activity or energy using DSM-IV-TR Criteria A and B items. The addition of increased activity/energy to Criterion A will make explicit the requirement that this hallmark symptom of bipolar I or II disorder needs to be present in order to make the diagnosis.   It may appear there is an overlap between the addition of “activity” to Criterion A and the Criterion B symptom of an “increase in goal-directed activity.” However, the Criterion A proposed change is a nonspecific, gateway inquiry about overall changes in activity or energy without the specific detailed and directed inquiry of Criterion B regarding goal-directed activity.

It is expected that the increased specificity will have a net null effect on prevalence, i.e., it will increase correct diagnosis and decrease incorrect diagnosis of bipolar disorder.  While the under recognition of bipolarity among patients diagnosed as having MDD has been documented, there is less information available on the “surge” in patients being diagnosed as having a bipolar disorder who, under expert examination, do not meet symptom criteria for hypomania or mania.  Given that the diagnosis of hypomania or mania may be met by, for example moodiness, spending sprees, increased or pressured speech, there is a lack of specificity.  We believe the addition of increased activity/energy will increase specificity of the application of this diagnosis.

Change 2: The work group is proposing the addition of a Mixed Features Specifier for Mania, Hypomania, and Depressive Episodes

 

A substantial proportion of individuals who seek treatment for symptoms that meet criteria for a major depressive episode (MDE) also describe an admixture of manic symptoms that are insufficient in number to meet the DSM IV definition of a mixed episode.  For those with no previous history of mania or hypomania, proportions with at least one manic symptom have ranged from 0.22 (1) to 0.50 (2).  Reported proportions with at least three symptoms have been 0.07 (3), 0.09 (1), and , 0.23 (4).  Similarly, proportions of 0.50 to 0.60 of individuals with bipolar I or II MDEs have had a least one coexisting manic symptom (3, 5) and approximately 11 to 16% describe at least three manic symptoms (3, 5).

The presence of sub-threshold manic symptoms has been associated with earlier onset (4,7), a greater number of episodes (4,7), higher likelihoods of past alcohol abuse and suicide attempts (8), more days with irritable or elevated mood in the preceding year (5), higher likelihoods of past rapid cycling (5), and a greater likelihood of a lifetime diagnosis of bipolar disorder (9).

Together with the substantial prevalence of subthreshold mixed states among individuals with MDEs is accumulating evidence that such states raise the likelihood that the presenting disorder reflects a bipolar diathesis.  Validating evidence derives from group differences in family history, age of onset, prior psychiatric history, diagnostic stability and measures of impulsivity.  The DSM IV recognizes only those with the coexistence of full manic and major depressive syndromes as having mixed states and thus fails to capture the important information conveyed by sub-threshold admixtures of manic symptoms.

Manic Episode

Note: This is not a codeable disorder.

A. A distinct period of abnormally and persistently elevated, expansive, or irritable mood, lasting at least 1 week (or any duration if hospitalization is necessary).

B. During the period of mood disturbance, three (or more) of the following symptoms have persisted (four if the mood is only irritable) and have been present to a significant degree:

1. Iinflated self-esteem or grandiosity

2. Decreased need for sleep (e.g., feels rested after only 3 hours of sleep)

3. More talkative than usual or pressure to keep talking

4. Flight of ideas or subjective experience that thoughts are racing

5. Distractibility (i.e., attention too easily drawn to unimportant or irrelevant external stimuli)

6. Increase in goal-directed activity (either socially, at work or school, or sexually) or psychomotor agitation

7. Excessive involvement in pleasurable activities that have a high potential for painful consequences (e.g., engaging in unrestrained buying sprees, sexual indiscretions, or foolish business investments)

C. The symptoms do not meet criteria for a Mixed Episode.

D. The mood disturbance is sufficiently severe to cause marked impairment in occupational functioning or in usual social activities or relationships with others, or to necessitate hospitalization to prevent harm to self or others, or there are psychotic features.

E. The symptoms are not due to the direct physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug of abuse, a medication, or other treatment) or a general medical condition (e.g., hyperthyroidism).

Note: Manic-like episodes that are clearly caused by somatic antidepressant treatment (e.g., medication, electroconvulsive therapy, light therapy) should not count toward a diagnosis of Bipolar I Disorder.
