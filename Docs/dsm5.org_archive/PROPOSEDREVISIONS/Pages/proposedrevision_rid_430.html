Cannabis Withdrawal

Updated April 30, 2012

 

A. Cessation of cannabis use that has been heavy and prolonged (i.e., usually daily or almost daily use over a period of at least a few months; however withdrawal symptoms have been observed among those with less frequent, but chronic use patterns)

B. Three (or more) of the following develop typically within a week after Criterion A:

1.      irritability, anger, or aggression

2.      nervousness or anxiety

3.      sleep difficulty (e.g., insomnia, disturbing dreams)  

4.      decreased appetite or weight loss

5.      restlessness

6.      depressed mood

7.      at least one of the following physical symptoms causing significant discomfort: stomach pain, shakiness/tremors, sweating, fever, chills, or headache

C. The symptoms in Criterion B cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

D. The symptoms are not associated with another medical condition and are not better accounted for by another psychiatric disorder.

 

Proposal to Include Cannabis Withdrawal Disorder and Withdrawal as a Criterion for Cannabis Dependence (Addiction)

 Introductory Summary Statement

Research on Cannabis Dependence and Withdrawal conducted since the publication of the DSM-IV clearly supports inclusion of Cannabis Withdrawal as a disorder and a Cannabis Dependence criterion in the DSM-5.  This manuscript reviews the scientific literature that provides the rationale and evidence to support this contention.  In addition, final choices for criteria selection and wording of those criteria encompass feedback from the DSM-5 Substance Use Workgroup members and another 10 experts who have published clinical and experimental studies on cannabis withdrawal.  In two review articles, Budney and colleagues provided clear documentation of the extant research that supports a compelling argument for the existence of a clinically important cannabis withdrawal syndrome (Budney & Hughes, 2006; Budney, et al., 2004).  Since those reviews were published, numerous other studies that included diverse clinical and general population samples have provided additional support for the contention that the cannabis withdrawal is a valid and reliable syndrome that can negatively impact abstinence attempts of heavy cannabis users.

Major points from the review articles are higlighted below, and briefly summarize findings from the more recent publications.  Based on this literature, criteria for a Cannabis Withdrawal Disorder are proposed and discussed.

What Constitutes a True Withdrawal Syndrome?

Hughes (Hughes, 1990) provided a comprehensive definition of a true drug withdrawal syndrome.  Specifically, a true drug withdrawal syndrome consists of a cluster of symptoms that: (1) are valid and reliably observed, and (2) have a clear time course that includes onset closely following cessation of the drug and a return to baseline levels.  The syndrome must be (3) pharmacologically specific to deprivation of the drug or one of its components, (4) not rare among dependent users, and (5) associated with clinically important consequences (e.g. contributes to relapse, or causes significant physical or psychological problems).

Status of Cannabis Withdrawal Based on Previous Reviews

The review articles by Budney and colleagues present evidence that the results of published cannabis withdrawal research are consistent with the criteria for a “true” withdrawal syndrome.  First, the reliability and validity have been demonstrated in experimental studies showing repeated withdrawal responses within the individual, and most importantly via demonstration of similar symptom profiles across studies conducted by different research groups and with diverse subject samples including adolescents, adults, general population samples, cannabis-using non-clinical and treatment seeking samples.  Second, studies have shown that the syndrome has a transient timecourse typical of other substance withdrawal syndromes.  Third, nonhuman and human laboratory studies provide clear evidence of the pharmacological specificity of the withdrawal syndrome.  That is, studies have demonstrated that deprivation of THC causes the symptoms, and that symptoms abate with re-administration of THC.  Moreover, preclinical studies have demonstrated precipitated withdrawal using a CB1 receptor antagonist, and the absence of both acute drug effects and precipitated withdrawal in CB1 receptor knockout mice exposed to THC or other CB1 receptor agonists further suggest pharmacological specificity.  Fourth, laboratory and clinical survey studies of heavy users show that the majority experience of participants show significant withdrawal symptoms indicating that the syndrome is not rare.  Although general population studies show much lower rates of cannabis withdrawal, the observed prevalence appears comparable to other substances with established withdrawal syndromes. 

Last, and perhaps the most controversial criterion for a true withdrawal syndrome is that it is clinically important.  Concern about the clinical significance of the cannabis withdrawal syndrome was a primary reason for its omission from the DSM-IV.  The DSM-IV TR text states:

“Symptoms of possible cannabis withdrawal (e.g., irritable or anxious mood accompanied by physiological changes such as tremor, perspiration, nausea, change in appetite, and sleep disturbances) have been described in association with the use of very high doses, but their clinical significance is uncertain.”

Evidence supporting the contention that the syndrome is clinically important has since emerged.  First, direct comparisons of cannabis withdrawal with tobacco withdrawal have consistently found that the magnitude and timecourse of the cannabis withdrawal effects appear comparable to the well-established tobacco withdrawal syndrome.  Second, cannabis users report using cannabis to “relieve withdrawal symptoms” suggesting that withdrawal might contribute to ongoing abuse of cannabis.  Third, a substantial proportion of adults and adolescents in treatment for cannabis dependence acknowledge moderate to severe withdrawal symptoms, and some complain that they make cessation more difficult.  In fact, cannabis users report having relapsed to cannabis use or initiating use of other drugs (e.g. tranquilizers) to provide relief from cannabis withdrawal symptoms.  Last, persons living with cannabis users observe significant withdrawal effects suggesting that such symptoms are disruptive to daily living.    

Recent Research not Included Budney et al. (2004; 2006)

Reliability and validity:  A prospective study of adolescents entering treatment for cannabis dependence clearly showed that adolescents experienced the “typical” withdrawal symptoms reported in prior retrospective studies, and that these symptoms were most severe during the first week of abstinence showing significant reductions over a 4 week course of abstinence (Milin, et al., 2008).  Additional reports of withdrawal in general population, clinical, and non-clinical samples are consistent with the type and duration of symptoms reported in the review papers (Agrawal, et al., 2008; Chung, et al., 2008; Copersino, et al., 2006; Hasin, et al., 2008).  Hasin et al. (2008) and Chung et al. (2008) reported a positive correlation between the number of withdrawal symptoms and DSM dependence severity (operationalized by number of criteria counts) in adult and adolescent clinical samples demonstrating the concurrent validity of cannabis withdrawal.  Such a relation is similar to what is observed with alcohol dependence. 

Increasing support has also emerged for the predictive validity of cannabis withdrawal severity.  Chung et al. (2008) reported that endorsement of at least 4 withdrawal symptoms by a clinical sample of substance abusing adolescents was predictive of cannabis dependence severity. Withdrawal severity also predicted cannabis dependence severity one year later.  Thus cannabis withdrawal appears to be a clear indicator of dependence severity and a predictor of a more chronic course.  Cornelius et al. (2008) reported that adolescents with greater cannabis withdrawal severity were also more likely to report rapid relapse to dependence.  In contrast, Arendt et al. (2007) reported that cannabis withdrawal severity at baseline did not predict relapse, however, because the assessment of relapse occurred on average 26 months post baseline and the sample size was quite small (n=36), interpretation of this result is difficult.

Prevalence: Additional evidence has accumulated demonstrating that cannabis withdrawal is not rare in the general population, and indeed is very common in persons seeking treatment for cannabis or among heavy users enrolled in research studies.  Two studies (Agrawal, et al., 2008; Hasin, et al., 2008) examined the prevalence of cannabis withdrawal symptoms using the NESARC data set.  Agrawal et al. (2008) reported that 29% of those who use cannabis use during the past year reported experiencing at least two cannabis withdrawal symptoms during the past year.  Hasin et al. (2008) reported that among those who had ever used cannabis greater than 3 times per week, 44% reported experiencing at least two cannabis withdrawal symptoms and 34% reported experiencing at least three.  A study of non-treatment seeking, but heavy cannabis using adults participating in a laboratory research projects reported that 81% of these participants reported experiencing at least two symptoms and 49% experienced at least four symptoms during past abstinence periods (Copersino, et al., 2006).  Chung et al. (2008) reported that a clinical sample of 214 adolescents reported an average of 4.4 withdrawal symptoms.  Cornelius et al. (2008) reported that cannabis withdrawal was endorsed by 92% of a clinical sample cannabis dependent adolescents (n=104) who also were also experiencing substantial depression.  These adolescents reported experiencing a mean of 6.0 symptoms with 73% reporting at least 4 symptoms.

Timecourse:  Two recent studies have provided additional data on the timecourse of the cannabis withdrawal syndrome.  Milin et al (2008) reported that prospective, weekly withdrawal assessments on 13 adolescents in either residential or day treatment indicated that withdrawal severity was greatest in the first two weeks of abstinence and continued into the third week.  Symptom counts and severity decreased over the course of 4 weeks.  Copersino et al. (2006) collected retrospective data on cannabis quit attempts from 104 non-treatment seeking adults.  Onset of “physical” symptoms were reported to have begun 1-3 days after cessation on average, while “psychological” symptoms began 2-10 days after cessation.  The physical symptoms lasted 2-19 days while the psychological lasted 5 weeks to 1 year.  Both these studies provide support for the transient timecourse of cannabis withdrawal.

Pharmacological Specificity: Budney et al (2007) observed that oral THC dose-dependently suppressed cannabis withdrawal in 8 heavy cannabis users participating in a within-subject laboratory study. These findings supported previous work by Haney and colleagues that had clearly demonstrated the pharmacological specificity of the syndrome. In a more recent study, Haney et al. (2008) provided additional data showing that oral THC suppressed cannabis withdrawal in a human laboratory experiment. 

Clinical Importance:  Additional data has also emerged to support the contention that the cannabis withdrawal syndrome has clinical importance.  Copersino et al. (2006) in their study of cannabis quitting experiences found that 56% of participants reported taking action to relieve their withdrawal symptoms, the most common action being to use a substance (e.g. initiation/increased use of tranquilizers or alcohol; resumed cannabis use).  The NESARC data also revealed evidence supporting the clinical significance of cannabis withdrawal.  Eight percent of recent cannabis users (past year use) experienced 2 or more withdrawal symptoms that caused impairment or reported using cannabis for withdrawal relief (Agrawal, et al., 2008).  Moreover, Hasin et al.’s (2008) analyses of the NESARC data revealed a strong relationship between withdrawal symptom count and reports of distress/impairment and between symptom count and reports of using a substance to relieve or reduce withdrawal.   Cornelius et al.’s (2008) observation that cannabis withdrawal was associated with rapid relapse to dependence provides support for the clinical significance of withdrawal among adolescents.  

Haney et al.’s (2008) human lab study demonstrated that medications that produced the greatest reduction in withdrawal (combination of Lofexidine and THC) also were most potent in reducing relapse in a laboratory model of relapse.  This suggests that withdrawal may be an important factor in the ability to obtain or maintain abstinence. 

Two studies provided further evidence indicating that cannabis withdrawal is comparable in severity to nicotine withdrawal, a syndrome with well-accepted clinical importance. Vandrey et al. (2007) conducted a within-subjects human lab study comparing cannabis and tobacco withdrawal in those who use both substances.  Overall withdrawal discomfort and individual withdrawal symptom severity during 5-day periods of abstinence were of similar magnitude.  A survey study of daily cannabis users and daily tobacco users who reported recent quit attempts again found highly similar levels of withdrawal severity in both groups (Budney, et al., 2008).  Moreover, both groups reported that withdrawal contributed substantially to relapse and the strength of these ratings was comparable.  

Last, the two studies reviewed above that showed some evidence of the predictive validity of cannabis withdrawal (worse outcomes associated with greater withdrawal) might also be considered as support for the syndrome’s clinical import (Chung, et al., 2008; Cornelius, et al., 2008).

Determining Criteria for Cannabis Withdrawal Disorder?

If Cannabis Withdrawal Disorder is to be included in the DSM-V, a list of symptoms must be selected and a minimal number of endorsed symptoms must be designated as necessary to meet criteria for having the Disorder.  Budney et al. (2004; 2006) suggested that the following list of symptoms be considered as part of the criteria for cannabis withdrawal disorder:

Common Symptoms:  

Anger/Aggression 

Decreased Appetite/Weight Loss                    

Irritability    

Anxiety/Nervousness   

Restlessness    

Sleep Difficulties /Strange Dreams                             

Less Common or Equivocal Symptoms 

Chills

Depressed Mood

Stomach Pain

Shakiness

Sweating  

The “common symptoms” proposed in this Table reflected those observed consistently across studies and reported by a substantial proportion of the participants studied.  The “less common or equivocal symptoms” were observed in some studies, were experienced by a minority of the study population, or were of lesser magnitude (Budney, et al., 2004).  To our knowledge, no published articles have asserted that these symptoms were too restrictive, inclusive, or inadequate.

Here, we briefly review the literature relevant to symptom item selection that was not included in the Budney et al. (2004; 2006) review papers.  The largest studies, with relevant information on specific symptoms were those that analyzed the NESARC data set (Agrawal, et al., 2008; Hasin, et al., 2008).  Of these two studies, Hasin et al. (2008) included the more relevant participants in their analyses, i.e., frequent cannabis users (5-7 days per week during heaviest use period) compared with the Agrawal et al. (2008) analyses that included those used any cannabis during the past year.  Hasin et al. (2008) reported the following to be the most frequently acknowledged withdrawal symptoms:  weak/tired (28%), yawning (25%), hypersomnia (25%), psychomotor retardation (21%), depressed mood (17%), anxiety (16%), sweating (9%), headaches (8%), restlessness (8%), insomnia (6%); and vivid dreams (7%).  As expected, Agrawal et al. (2008) reported similar symptoms acknowledged at lower rates, but in similar rank order.  Interestingly, these two general population-based studies reported high prevalence of a number of symptoms not on the Budney et al. (2004; 2006) list:  weak/tired, yawning, hypersomnia, psychomotor retardation, appetite increase, and headaches.

Three other studies with moderate size populations of cannabis abusers have also reported prevalence data on specific symptoms.  The retrospective reports from Copersino et al.’s (2006) sample of 104 adult non-treatment seekers indicated symptom prevalence rates as follows:  craving (66%), irritability (48%), difficulty sleeping (32%), anxiety (32%), increased appetite (25%), decreased appetite (23%) depressed mood (18%), physical discomfort (10%).  Here, increased appetite was unexpectedly reported by a substantial number of participants.

Chung et al’s (2008) adolescent sample reported the following symptom prevalence: craving (66%), irritability (46%), trouble concentrating (38%); difficulty sleeping (34%), restless (33%); anger (28%), change in appetite (28%), anxiety (26%), headache (26%), depressed mood (25%), tired/sleepy (23%), yawning (21%), vivid/unpleasant dreams (15%).  Note that trouble concentrating, headache, tired/sleep, and yawning are items not on the Budney et al. (2004; 2006) list, and change in appetite could reflect increased or decreased appetite. 

Last, Budney et al.’s (2008) sample of 67 adults who made a cannabis quit attempt within the last 30 days reported symptom rates consistent with the Budney et al. (2004; 2006) list, however, they also reported relatively substantial rates of the following items:  difficulty concentrating (60%), headache (46%), and increased appetite (26%).

Two studies have also performed factor analyses of the cannabis withdrawal symptoms to provide further understanding of the nature of this syndrome.  Hasin et al’s (2008) analyses of the NESARC data set showed revealed a two-factor solution.  One factor was labeled Weakness and included hypersomnia, weak or tired, yawning, and psychomotor retardation.  The other factor was labeled Depression/Anxiety and included depressed mood, sweating, nausea, anxiety, muscle aches, restless, tremors, insomnia.  Copersino et al. (2006) performed a factor analysis on their 104 adult, heavy cannabis users who were non-treatment seeking.  They reported a PCA solution also with two primary factors.  One was labeled a Physical and included physical discomfort, tremor muscle twitches, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and upset stomach.  The second was labeled Psychological and included anxiety, irritability, difficulty sleeping, depression, and boredom.  A third factor accounting for 9% of the variance was referred to as related to Drive States and included craving, increase in appetite, and decreased sex drive. 

Issues to be Determined

(1) How do we select the symptom items to include in a criteria list for cannabis withdrawal?

(2) How do we decide on how many symptoms must be reported to meet criteria for the disorder?

Symptom Selection

Guiding Principles.  A conservative approach to selection of symptoms would seem most prudent.  This approach presumes that adding symptoms in the future if new research suggests such an action, would be more readily achieved and acceptable than removing symptoms from the list.  Trying to select items with evidence from both controlled laboratory studies and clinical survey/interview studies would appear a rationale method for development of this first version of the criteria.  Symptoms or signs that have some data to support their validity, but not enough to provide a high degree of confidence, can be mentioned as possible symptoms of cannabis withdrawal in the text that describes the disorder.  This would alert clinicians to such symptoms and allow them to consider their importance for individual cases.  Discussing these in the text may also prompt future research to more thoroughly examine their potential as true withdrawal criteria.

Symptoms with Strong Evidence

The following symptoms have substantial and robust evidence from laboratory and clinical studies suggesting their inclusion in the criteria list.

Irritability:  Budney et al. (2004) review noted support in 14 of 16 studies (lab and clinical).  Studies published since provide further support, although the large sample NESARC analyses did not include this item in the assessment of withdrawal.

Anger /Aggression:  Budney et al. (2004) review noted support in 6 of 11 studies (lab and clinical) for increased anger or aggression. The two clinical survey studies published since also showed support for significant rates of reporting of this symptom. 

A question regarding these first three symptom criteria was whether or not they should be combined and included as one criterion.  They could each reflect expression of the same underlying construct perhaps along a severity dimension, or reflect individual differences in expression of the construct.  Feedback and comments on this issue from the DSM-V committee and additional cannabis withdrawal experts was somewhat mixed, but generally favored combining these items.  Given that there is precedent from the current Nicotine Withdrawal Disorder criteria for combing these types of symptoms, we would recommend including these as one criterion.  This would allow clinicians some latitude in determining if the patient’s symptom report meets this criterion, and would prevent attributing too much significance this type of withdrawal response if irritability, anger, and aggression are related expressions of the same underlying construct.

Anxiety / Nervousness:  The Budney et al. (2004) review noted support in 12 of 16 studies (lab and clinical).  Again, studies published since provide further support for including this symptom.  Feedback was very minimal on this criterion reflecting a general consensus for inclusion as is.

Sleep Difficulty:  The Budney et al. (2004) review noted support in 13 of 16 studies (lab and clinical) for sleep difficulties, and 4 of 4 supporting “strange” dreams.  Studies published since provide further support, although the large sample NESARC analyses reported a moderate prevalence of hypersomnia and relatively low prevalence of insomnia.  Previous clinical and lab studies have reported “sleep difficulty” which typically would be more related to insomnia or difficulty sleeping rather than hypersomnia. 

Two issues with this criterion is whether or not to include hypersomnia and whether to combine sleep difficulty and strange dreams into a single criterion on the criteria list or omit strange dreams and discuss it in the text only.  Regarding hypersomnia, the experimental data clearly does not support its inclusion.  One possible explanation for why hypersomnia is acknowledged in the general population and survey studies is the delayed retrospective reporting methods used in those studies.  Hypersomnia may occur after an initial period of insomnia, and perhaps reflect what has been called a post-acute withdrawal symptom in the context of other substance withdrawal syndromes.  Feedback on this issue was mixed.  Some suggested that labeling the criterion, “sleep difficulty”, would encompass either insomnia or hypersomnia.  Given the lack of adequate prospective, experimental studies, we recommend using the term, sleep difficulty.  Feedback on the other issue, where to include “strange” dreams, reflected a general consensus to include a description of what has been observed about dreaming in the text only, as we await additional research on the nature of this phenomenon.

Decreased Appetite or Weight Loss:  Budney et al. review (2004) noted support in 15 of 18 studies (lab and clinical) for decreased appetite, and 5 of 7 mostly lab studies providing support for weight loss.  Studies published since offer support for this, but also provide some evidence of increased appetite.  Of note, Copersino et al’s (2006) data suggest that increased appetite onsets after a period of decreased appetite, perhaps indicating a perceived enhanced appetite following the initial withdrawal effect of decreased appetite.  The laboratory studies indicate that decreased appetite and weight loss are transient (usually dissipate within 1 week), supporting the notion that the retrospective reports of increased appetite as a withdrawal syndrome might actually reflect perceptions of what occurs following the cannabis withdrawal syndrome (i.e., regaining their appetite lost during withdrawal).  Although some feedback suggestion using the term, appetite change, most thought the experimental data was strong indicating the term, decreased appetite, should be used.  Given these data, we recommend using the term decrease appetite and combining with, or weight loss.  This would be consistent with how the terms, increased appetite and weight gain are combined for Nicotine Withdrawal.  The possibility of increased appetite will be mentioned in the text as a possible post-withdrawal symptom.

Restlessness:  Budney et al. (2004) review noted support in 11 of 15 studies (lab and clinical) for restlessness.  Studies published since provide further support, although the large sample NESARC analyses found relatively low prevalence rates (3%-8%).  Little feedback was received on this symptom, indicating consensus that it should be included as is. 

Symptoms with Questionable or Less Support

Depressed Mood:  The Budney et al. (2004) review noted support in 9 of 16 studies (lab and clinical) for depressed mood.  Studies published since offer additional support.  Approximately 15-65% of participants across the survey studies report depressed mood as a withdrawal symptom, although Arendt et al. (2007) who also obtained measures at a follow-up time point did not find increased ratings of depressed mood during withdrawal compared with follow-up ratings.  The primary reason that depressed mood is in the questionable support category is the lack of clear evidence in the laboratory studies that obtained careful baseline data. (Budney, et al., 2001; Budney, et al., 2003; Haney, Comer, et al., 1999; Haney, Ward, et al., 1999).  Nonetheless, the majority of feedback suggested that depressed mood should be included, citing that it appears robust in clinical populations.  Given this, we recommend including depressed mood as a symptom criterion.

Physical Symptoms:  Budney et al.’s  (2004) review noted support in 6 of 12 studies (lab and clinical) for stomach pains/physical symptoms, 5 of 12 studies for sweating, and 5 of 12 studies for tremor/shakiness, and 5 of 12 for chills.  Studies published since provide limited support for these symptoms.  The NESARC analyses (Hasin, et al., 2008) show prevalence rates below 5% for nausea, 9% for sweating, 4% for tremors and did not assess chills.  Chung et al. (2008) reported 3% nausea, 3% sweating, 8% tremors, and no data on chills.  Copersino et al., (2006) reported rates of 10% for physical discomfort, 3% for nausea, 6% upset stomach, and 4% for tremors.  Budney et al. (2007) reported rates of 20% for stomach pains, 18% sweating, and 35% shakiness.  Arendt et al. (2007) reported additional evidence for sweating, chills, and shakiness. Note that when severity of these physical symptoms has been measured, the severity rating is quite low, typically acknowledged as mild (Arendt, et al., 2007; Budney, et al., 2003; Budney, et al., 1999; Budney, et al., 2008).

Not included on the proposed physical symptom criteria list from Budney et al. (2004) was headache.  Headache was not reviewed in the Budney et al. (2004; 2006) review papers.  Clinical survey reports have revealed mixed findings.  Headaches have been reported as a withdrawal symptom by over 40% of adults attempting to quit cannabis use (Budney et al. 1999; 2008).  Arendt et al. (2007) reported that 30% acknowledged headache as a symptom, but this rate of headache was not significantly different than follow-up.  Chung et al. (2008) reported 26%, Cornelius et al. (2008) reported 27%, and Vandrey et al. (2005) reported 32% of their adolescents acknowledging headaches during cannabis withdrawal. Hasin et al. (2008) reported that 8% and Agrawal et al. (2008) reported 5% in their subsamples of cannabis users from the NESARC data set.  Of note, as with depressed mood, that the laboratory studies that have assessed headache have not observed clear evidence to support it as a cannabis withdrawal symptom, but clinical and general population samples suggest it warrants inclusion.

Feedback on including physical symptoms formed a clear consensus indicating that a physical symptom criterion should be included.  Ideas on how to structure this criterion item were mixed, however, there was some consensus that a list of symptoms should be provided and either 1 or 2 of those symptoms would need to be reported to meet that criterion.  Although there was some argument that 2 symptoms should be required because the symptoms are fairly non-specific, most experts that responded suggested that we require just one physical symptom that causes significant distress. 

Fatigue / Yawning: Fatigue and yawning also were not reviewed as specific items in the Budney reviews.  However, survey studies have provided some evidence for these symptoms.  The Hasin et al. (2008) reported prevalence rates of 25% for yawning and 28% for fatigue (weak/tired).  Chung et al. (2008) reported 21% for yawning and 23% for tired/sleepy; while Cornelius reported 22% for yawning and 28% for tired/sleepy among their cannabis dependent adolescents.  An earlier report by Crowley et al. (1998), also found support for yawning and fatigue among cannabis dependent adolescents, approximately 40% and 55% reporting these symptoms respectively.  Of note, much of the support for these symptoms comes from adolescent survey studies.   Feedback provided a clear consensus that these two items be discussed in the text, but not included in the criteria list, primarily because evidence comes mostly from adolescent samples and these symptoms may be associated with sleep difficulty.

Trouble Concentrating: Difficulty concentrating has been reported at a high prevalence rate among the adolescent clinical samples and one adult sample (38%-60%).  However, no evidence has emerged from the laboratory and no data are available from the large general population samples such as NESARC.  Feedback was again almost unanimous in suggesting this symptom not be included because of a lack of empirical support and because trouble concentrating is also considered a direct effect of cannabis use.

Craving: Craving is acknowledged as a withdrawal symptom in the majority of studies, but experimental studies have not uniformly demonstrated that it is a true withdrawal symptom as indicated by early onset, and a transient timecourse.  That is, substance dependent individuals crave the substance while using and while attempting to abstain.  The DSM-IV acknowledged that most if not all individuals in Withdrawal (for any substance) experience craving for the specific substance.   However, craving is not included in any substance withdrawal syndrome because of high baseline rates (i.e., also experienced regularly during periods of use) and measurement issues.  Feedback on craving indicated that we should not include it as a criterion unless the Committee plans to consider it for all substance withdrawal syndromes.

Number of Items Needed to Meet Criteria

The DSM-5 also needs to specify the number of criterion items that are needed to meet criteria for Cannabis Withdrawal Disorder.  There is limited data available to guide this decision.  Relevant information that could be helpful is review of the few studies that provide prevalence data related to the rates of occurrence of various numbers of symptoms reported in clinical and general population samples, and consideration of the algorithms used for withdrawal disorders already included in the DSM-IV.

Research on Number of Symptoms.   In an early study, Budney et al. (1999) reported that 57% of adults seeking treatment for cannabis dependence report 6 or more symptoms of at least moderate severity and 47% reported at least 4 symptoms rated as severe.  Vandrey et al. (2005) reported that 57% of adolescents seeking treatment for marijuana report 6 or more symptoms of at least moderate severity and 47% reported at least 4 symptoms rated as severe.  Note, however, in both these studies, irritability and anger/aggression, and sleep difficulty/strange dreams, each count as separate symptoms and craving was also counted as a symptom.  Chung et al. (2008) reported that 37% of substance abusing, treatment-seeking adolescents reported 2 or more symptoms and 18% reported 4 or more of the 6 most common symptoms identified in Budney et al. 2004.  Note that craving was not included as one of the commons symptoms, and anger/aggression and insomnia/strange dreams were treated as combined symptom items.  Cornelius et al (2008) reported that 73% of depressed, cannabis dependent adolescents reported at least 4 symptoms using withdrawal symptom counts similar to Budney et al. (1999) and Vandrey et al. (2005).  Copersino et al. (2006) reported that 49% of adults, non-treatment seekers report experiencing at least 4 symptoms, however this list included “boredom”, increased sex drive and craving.  Hasin et al. (2008) found that 34% of adults in the NESARC sample at least 3 symptoms.  Note that in this study, irritability was not included as a symptom, nor was craving.

Regarding concurrent validity, the Hasin study found that reporting at least 3 withdrawal symptoms was related to greater duration of and greater quantity of cannabis use, and found some indication of earlier onset of cannabis use.  Interestingly, Hasin et al. also reported that the rate of cannabis dependence in this NESARC subsample was 16% (omitting use of the withdrawal criterion for making dependence diagnoses); however this number increased to 32% if a withdrawal criterion was used to make the diagnosis (at least 2 symptoms), and increased to 30% if an at least 3 symptom criterion was used.  Regarding predictive validity, Chung et al. (2008) reported that experiencing at least 4 symptoms, but not experiencing at least 2 symptoms predicted number of dependence symptoms one year later in a clinical sample of substance abusing adolescents.  Thus, cannabis withdrawal appears to be a clear indicator of dependence severity and a predictor of a more chronic course.

Unfortunately, these existing data do not provide as meaningful information as one would like because the symptom lists used across studies varied in number and type.  Therefore, the number of symptoms acknowledged in each study are not based on the criteria list proposed in this paper.

DSM-IV withdrawal criteria lists for other substances.  Decision about how many withdrawal symptom items to require to meet the diagnosis might also be guided by using the current DSM Substance Withdrawal Disorders as a model.  Below is a list of the extant withdrawal disorders and the number of symptoms in the criteria list and the number of symptoms required to diagnose a withdrawal disorder.

As indicated by the Table, a fairly wide range exists for both the number of items listed (4-9) and the number of symptoms required for diagnosis (2-4).  Also, three of the disorders require one “hallmark” symptom plus other symptoms.  Hence, there is no clear suggestion to be gleaned from the Table.

Feedback from the experts reflected a general suggestion to go with fewer (2 or 3) symptoms expressing concern about missed cases.  One expert suggested a more conservative approach, i.e., must meet at least half of the total number criteria (4).  At this time, we would suggest using the 3 or more criteria, which would reflect a moderate approach to making the diagnosis.

 

Tentative Proposal for DSM-5

Based on the existing literature on cannabis withdrawal and the criteria structure of the current substance withdrawal disorders, we propose the following: 

A.  Cessation of cannabis that has been heavy and prolonged.

B.  Three (or more) of the following develop within several days after Criterion A:

            (1) irritability, anger, or increased aggression

            (2) nervousness or anxiety

            (3) sleep difficulty (insomnia)

            (4) decreased appetite or weight loss

            (5) restlessness

            (6) depressed mood

(7) at least one of the following physical symptoms  causing significant discomfort (stomach pain, shakiness/tremors, sweating, fever, chills, headache)                

C.  The symptoms in Criterion B cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

D.  The symptoms are not due to a general medical condition and are not better accounted for by another disorder. 

Note:  Text will discuss: disturbing/strange dreams, fatigue, yawning, difficulty concentrating, and possible reports of increased appetite following a period of decreased appetite during the early stages of withdrawal.
