Neurobehavioral Disorder Associated with Prenatal Alcohol Exposure

Update May 1, 2012

 

This condition is being recommended for further study in Section III, which is the section of the DSM-5 text in which conditions that require further research will be included.

 

A.     More than minimal exposure to alcohol at any time during gestation, including prior to pregnancy recognition. Confirmation of gestational exposure to alcohol may be obtained from any of the following sources: maternal self-report of alcohol use in pregnancy, collateral reports, or medical or other records.

 

B.     Neurocognitive impairment, as evidenced by one (or more) of the following:

1.      global intellectual impairment (i.e., IQ of 70 or below, or a standard score of 70 or below on a comprehensive developmental assessment)

 

2.      impairment in executive functioning (e.g., poor planning and organization; difficulty changing strategies or inflexibility; difficulty with behavioral inhibition)

 

3.      impairment in learning (e.g., lower academic achievement than expected for intellectual level; requires special education services; specific learning disability)

 

4.      impairment in memory (e.g., problems remembering information learned recently; repeatedly making the same mistakes; difficulty remembering lengthy verbal instructions)

 

5.      impairment in visual spatial reasoning (e.g., disorganized or poorly planned drawings or constructions; problems differentiating left from right; problems aligning numbers in columns)

 

C.     Impairment in self-regulation in one (or more) of the following:

1.      impairment in mood or behavioral regulation (e.g., mood lability; negative affect or irritability; frequent behavioral outbursts)

 

2.      attention deficit (e.g., difficulty encoding new information; difficulty shifting attention; difficulty sustaining mental effort)

 

3.      impairment in impulse control (e.g., difficulty waiting turn; difficulty complying with rules; confabulating; taking possessions of others)

 

D.     Deficits in adaptive functioning as manifested in two (or more) of the following, including at least one of (1) or (2):

1.      communication deficit (e.g., delayed acquisition of language; difficulty understanding spoken language; difficulty using language to express self so that the listener understands)

 

2.      social impairment (e.g., overly friendly with strangers; difficulty reading social cues; difficulty understanding social consequences; acting “too young”)

 

3.      impairment in daily living (e.g., delayed toileting, feeding, or bathing; problems following rules of personal safety; difficulty managing daily schedule)

 

4.      motor impairment (e.g., poor fine motor development; delayed attainment of gross motor milestones or ongoing deficits in gross motor function; problems in coordination and balance)

 

E.     The onset of the disturbance (symptoms in Criteria B, C, and D) is before 18 years of age.

 

F.     The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

 

G.    The disturbance is not better explained by the direct physiological effects associated with postnatal use of a substance (e.g., medication, alcohol or other drugs), another medical condition (e.g., traumatic brain injury, delirium, dementia), other known teratogens (e.g., Fetal Hydantoin syndrome), a genetic condition (e.g., Williams syndrome, Down syndrome, Cornelia de Lange syndrome), or environmental neglect.

Rationale for the Inclusion of Neurobehavioral Disorder Associated with Prenatal Alcohol Exposure in Section III of the DSM-5 

 

1.  A Need for the Category

Alcohol is a neurobehavioral teratogen, and a large body of experimental animal research, longitudinal human studies and clinical research all document the teratogenic effects of prenatal alcohol exposure (PAE) on fetal growth and development and on central nervous system function and behavior throughout the lifespan. 

This extensive research has shown that PAE results in a set of diagnostic conditions that fall on a spectrum, a point that was also made in the original definition of alcohol-related conditions by the Institute of Medicine (Stratton, Howe, & Battaglia, 1996).  Accordingly, these have collectively been labeled as Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD or FASDs).  The term “fetal alcohol spectrum disorders” was accepted in 2004 by consensus of governmental, research and advocacy organizations (http://www.nofas.org/advocate/terminology.aspx ) and is considered a global public health concern.  FASD, as an umbrella term, includes the more specific diagnostic conditions of what the Institute of Medicine previously termed Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS), partial FAS, Alcohol Related Neurodevelopmental Disorder (ARND), and Alcohol Related Birth Defects (ARBD). 

Although the IOM’s diagnostic coding schema has been useful in delineating the variation in the physical characteristics associated with PAE, it does not adequately provide a method of describing the neurodevelopmental and mental health symptoms associated with PAE. As a result, the service needs of many children with PAE are not identified or are often inappropriately treated by mental health professionals. The term Neurobehavioral Disorder Associated with Prenatal Alcohol Exposure (ND-PAE) is a new clarifying term, intended to encompass the full range of developmental disabilities associated with exposure to alcohol in utero. The current diagnostic guidelines allow ND-PAE to be diagnosed both in the presence or absence of the physical effects of PAE.

Individuals diagnosed with ND-PAE have brain damage that can result in a variety of neurodevelopmental outcomes with individual variation in their presentation. This is consistent with extensive data on the mechanisms of alcohol as a neurobehavioral teratogen (Goodlett, Horn, & Zhou, 2005; Guerri, Bazinet, & Riley, 2009; Guerri, Pascual, & Renau-Piqueras, 2001; Weinberg, Sliwowska, Lan, & Hellemans, 2008). There are several comprehensive literature reviews on these outcomes (Kable & Coles, 2004b; Kodituwakku, 2009a; Mattson, Vaurio, et al., 2010; Riley & McGee, 2005). 

The omission from the DSM of a diagnosis describing this constellation of impairments has likely contributed to the difficulty many of these individuals and their families experience in accessing appropriate services for mental health issues.  Of particular concern, individuals impacted by PAE are significantly over-represented yet under-recognized in the child welfare system, juvenile detention and correctional facilities, and inpatient psychiatric settings.  Many of these individuals are seen in outpatient mental health settings, but are often not appropriately identified.  Consequently, these individuals are frequently treated inappropriately (often to their considerable detriment) by mental health, educational, and criminal justice systems.  Previous research on outcomes for individuals with  a history of PAE, but who did not meet full criteria for FAS, has found them to be at higher risk for a number of adverse life outcomes, including delinquency, school failure, and alcohol and drug abuse problems than individuals who did meet full criteria for FAS.  Such findings may seem surprising, but this pattern may occur in part because these individuals are less readily identified as needing services, and are less likely to qualify for and receive services than the much smaller group of individuals who meet full criteria for FAS.  The inclusion of a diagnosis of ND-PAE in the DSM-5 would undoubtedly facilitate recognition of individuals negatively impacted by PAE who may be in need of treatment, regardless of whether or not they demonstrate the associated physical sequelae of dysmorphic facial features and growth impairment.  

2.   Relationship with Other DSM-5 Diagnoses

Children with ND-PAE have been found to have a higher incidence of oppositional defiant (ODD) and conduct disorder (CD) symptoms (Disney, Iacono, McGue, Tully, & Legrand, 2008; Walthall, O'Connor, & Paley, 2008). Clinicians making a dual diagnosis of ND-PAE and ODD or CD should make sure that the symptoms associated with ODD and CD are not primarily a reflection of the brain damage or lower cognitive functioning seen in ND-PAE. For example, poor comprehension of requests or commands may sometimes appear to be noncompliant behavior, confabulation may be mistaken for lying, and poor understanding of ownership may result in a child taking things that do not belong to them. Symptoms of depression and mania (Famy, Streissguth, & Unis, 1998; Fryer, McGee, Matt, Riley, & Mattson, 2007; O'Connor & Kasari, 2000; O'Connor, Kogan, & Findlay, 2002; Walthall, et al., 2008) have been reported as well as an increased incidence of somatoform disorder, substance dependence or abuse disorders, and paranoid, passive-aggressive, and antisocial personality disorders in adults with PAE (Barr et al., 2006; Yates, Cadoret, Troughton, Stewart, & Giunta, 1998) but the most common mental health diagnosis given to individuals with ND-PAE is ADHD (Peadon & Elliott, 2010). Table 1 contains fourteen published studies comparing children with a history of PAE to those with ADHD. Although initially similarities were identified between the two disorders (Nanson and Hiscock, 1990), examination of the two disorders over the years has resulted in the conclusion that ND-PAE and ADHD have distinctive neuropsychological characteristics with some symptom overlap.

 

In a careful review of the literature comparing children with PAE and children with ADHD, Mattson and her colleagues (Mattson, Crocker, & Nguyen, 2011) concluded that children with PAE had more difficulty with problem-solving, verbal fluency, overall IQ, shifting attention, verbal encoding, daily living skills, and face and emotion processing than do children with ADHD. In contrast, children with ADHD have more difficulties with basic motor control, focused attention, sustained attention, and retrieval than do children with PAE.  Symptom overlap between the disorders was identified in the areas of set shifting, complex motor skills, static balance, social skills, communication skills, and parental report of behavioral problems. In addition, children with PAE may differ from children with ADHD in key aspects of executive functioning involved in modulating relationships between attention and arousal level.  Using a sustained attention task, children with PAE were found to have more difficulties with conditions associated with over-arousal; whereas, individuals with ADHD demonstrated deficits in the under-arousal condition (Kooistra, et al., 2010). Individuals with ADHD were also found to differ from those with PAE in the pattern of their neural processing using event-related potentials as outcome measures while engaged in an inhibition task (Burden, et al., 2010).

Studies assessing the response of children with PAE to standard pharmacological treatments commonly used to treat children with ADHD have produced mixed results. Table 2 contains human and animal model studies assessing the impact of pharmacological Interventions on attention and activity level. A fixed dose crossover study of four children with ND-PAE, (Oesterheld, et al., 1998) found positive effects of methylphenidate compared to placebo on parent and teacher ratings of hyperactive and impulsive symptoms, but no such effects for objective measures of inattention.  In another crossover study of 11 children with PAE and ADHD, Snyder, Nanson, Snyder, and Block (1997) found significant effects for stimulants compared to placebo on parent reports of hyperactive symptoms, but no improvement in measures of attention or impulsivity.  Retrospective studies of medication response among individuals with PAE have also yielded mixed results.  O’Malley, Koplin, and Dohner (2000) reported that in a sample of children diagnosed with FAS/ARND and ADHD, 79% of patients given dextroamphetamine showed a positive clinical response, whereas only 22% of the patients who were given methylphenidate showed a positive clinical response.  In a retrospective chart review of 22 patients with FASDs, Coe and colleagues (2001) found 63% exhibited positive effects of stimulant medications on symptoms of impulsivity, inattentiveness, or hyperactivity but approximately one third of the trials had to be stopped due to concerns regarding negative side effects.  Most recently, Doig and colleagues (2008) reviewed 41 medication trials in a sample of 27 children with PAE and ADHD.  The majority of the trials consisted of stimulant medications, although some included combinations of psychostimulants with other medications, such as clonidine, risperidone, or carbamazepine. Significant effects were seen on all three domains in the hyperactive/impulsive and oppositional/defiant but to a lesser extent in the inattentive domain.  Collectively, these suggest that inattentive symptoms are not effectively ameliorated by psychostimulant medications in children with FASD.

Animal models of PAE suggested reactivity to methylphenidate in the postnatal period is altered (Means, Medlin, Hughes, & Gray, 1984; Shen & Choong, 2006; Ulug & Riley, 1983), resulting in increased activity and decreased excitability of the dopamine neurons in the ventral tegmental area (Shen & Choong, 2006).  In contrast, reduced activity and improved performance on learning tasks have been found in response to choline supplementation delivered prenatally (Thomas et al., 2004) or postnatally (Thomas et al., 2007). The use of fenofibrate, which is lipid regulating agent that affects the nuclear receptors (i.e. peroxisome proliferator-actived receptor-PPAR-alpha), has also been found to minimize symptoms of hyperactivity after PAE but the impact of these substances with humans with ND-PAE is not yet known.

Finally, the differential diagnosis of ND-PAE includes cognitive impairment or intellectual disability in the absence of PAE and several genetic and teratogenic conditions that share some common features with the disorder. Genetic conditions such as Cornelia de Lange Syndrome, Down Syndrome, and Dubowitz Syndrome and other teratogenic conditions such as Fetal Hydantoin Syndrome and Maternal Phenylketonuria may have similar physical characteristics to those seen in some children with PAE.  A careful review of prenatal exposure history is needed to clarify the teratogenic agent and an evaluation by a geneticist trained in dysmorphology may be needed to distinguish physical characteristics associated with these conditions from those commonly seen in children with ND-PAE.  Other considerations may include the direct physiological effects of use or misuse of a substance such as medication, alcohol or other drugs, or problems attributed to a general medical condition such as traumatic brain injury or other neurocognitive disorders (i.e. delirium, dementia). A careful history of the onset of the symptoms and current substance use should aid in differentiating these conditions from ND-PAE.

3.  Available Treatments

Recommendations for treatment approaches are available for those who would be recognized with ND-PAE with this new diagnostic category (Goodlett, et al., 2005; Kalberg & Buckley, 2007; Olson, et al., 2007).  Existing treatment approaches have been critically reviewed  (Chandrasena, Mukherjee, & Turk, 2009; Hagerman, 1999; Paley & O'Connor, 2009; Peadon, Rhys-Jones, Bower, & Elliott, 2009; Premji, Benzies, Serrett, & Hayden, 2007).  Promising approaches to improvement of neurobehavioral outcomes are being explored in animal models and clinical samples (Adnams et al., 2007; Coles, Strickland, Padgett, & Bellmoff, 2007; Goodlett, et al., 2005; Kable, Coles, & Taddeo, 2007; O'Connor et al., 2006; Olson, et al., 2004; Olson & Montague, in press; Olson, Oti, Gelo, & Beck, 2009; Thomas, Abou, & Dominguez, 2009). 

Several lines of animal research suggest the promise of various prenatal and neonatal interventions.  The benefits of prenatal and postnatal treatment with neuroprotective peptides in mitigating the effects of PAE on brain development have been reported (Incenti et al., 2010a, b; Zhou, Fang, & Goodlett, 2008).  Studies have also documented the protective effects of various nutrients (Naseer, Lee, & Kim, 2010; Ojeda et al., 2009; Serrano, Han, Brinez, & Linask, 2010; Summers, Rofe, & Coyle, 2009; Thomas, Abou, & Domnguez, 2009).  Other research has demonstrated positive effects of neonatal handling, postnatal environment enrichment, and rehabilitative training on rats and mice with perinatal alcohol exposure (Guerri et al., 2005; Thomas, Sather, & Whinery, 2008).   This animal research has direct implications for clinical intervention with humans, and pilot studies to examine environmental enrichment and motor training interventions are underway now.

Studies of treatments for children with PAE have targeted behavioral problems, cognitive and academic skills, and adaptive skills.  Positive effects have been demonstrated in studies utilizing supportive behavioral consultation with parents (see Betrand, 2009), socio-cognitive habilitation to improve math skills (Coles, Kable, & Taddeo, 2009; Kable, Coles, & Taddeo, 2007), language and literacy training (Adnams et al., 2007), rehearsal training to improve working memory (Loomes et al., 2008),  a computer-based intervention to increase fire and street safety skills (Coles, Strickland, Padgett, & Bellmoff, 2007), and a manualized, parent-assisted social skills intervention (O’Connor et al., 2006).

Community and clinic-based surveys indicate that stimulants are commonly used for children with PAE (O’Malley and Nanson, 2002).  Clinical trials using standard ADHD medications have begun but so far produced mixed results. Two crossover studies utilizing small samples demonstrated positive effects of stimulants on hyperactive symptoms, but not on measures of inattention (Oesterheld et al., 1998; Snyder, Nanson, Snyder, & Block, 1997).  Retrospective studies of medication response among individuals with PAE have also yielded mixed results, with some research finding more positive responses to some stimulant medications than others (O’Malley, Koplin, & Dohner, 2000), or a significant number of side effects (Coe et al., 2001). Most recently, Doig and colleagues (2008) reviewed 41 medication trials in a sample of 27 children with PAE and ADHD.  Significant effects were seen on all three domains of the MTA-SNAP-IV, although more children received normalized scores in the hyperactive/impulsive and oppositional/defiant domains than in the inattentive domain.  Collectively, these suggest that inattentive symptoms may not be effectively ameliorated by psychostimulant medications. Psychostimulant medications have also not been found to be as beneficial as neuroleptics in facilitating response to cognitive behavioral treatments (Frankel, Paley, Marquardt, & O'Connor, 2006).  Clearly larger samples and more intensive research attention is needed, which would be facilitated by recognition of the ND-PAE diagnostic category.

4.  Meets Criteria for a Mental (Psychiatric) Diagnosis

As outlined in the current memorandum and associated appendices, the proposed new condition, ND-PAE, meets the criteria for a mental (psychiatric) disorder as delineated in Stein et al. (2010).  The condition is a behavioral, psychological syndrome that occurs in an individual. It creates clinically significant distress and impairment, and is not merely an expectable response to stress or loss— or a culturally sanctioned response to a particular event— nor is it the result of social deviance or societal conflicts. ND-PAE is associated with psychobiological dysfunction. With regard to other considerations as presented by Stein et al. (2010), the current document and associated appendices summarize data on validity and clinical utility. These materials also consider differentiation from “nearest” neighbors, specifically ADHD.

Severity criteria for this disorder have not been proposed.
This disorder is not listed in DSM-IV; therefore, DSM-IV criteria for this disorder do not exist.
