Sleep Terror Disorder

  • Proposed  Revision
  • Rationale
  • Severity
  • DSM-IV

The work group is recommending that this disorder be subsumed into a new disorder: Disorder of Arousal.

Formerly Sleepwalking and Sleep Terrors were listed as indificual parasomnias. Inasmuch as "confusional arousals", "sleepwalking", and "sleep terrors" represent varying degrees of wake/NREM sleep admixture and represent variations of a single underlying pathophysiology, it is proposed to have a single parasomnia heading of "Disorders of Arousal" with three "subtypes": "confusional arousals", "sleepwalking", and "sleep terrors". Sleepwalking and sleep terrors – along with confusional arousals represent variations of the same physiological phenomenon

Validators: almost always familial, characteristic PSG findings during an event, SPECT studies in one case of sleepwalking

Relationship to International Classification of Diseases-10

Sleepwalking (somnambulism) F51.3, Sleep terrors (night terrors) F51.4,

International Classification of Sleep Disorders 2nd Edition

The ICSD lists each of the Arousal Disorder subtypes (Confusional Arousals, Sleepwalking, and Sleep Terrors) as separate parasomnias under the heading of "Disorders of Arousal (From NREM Sleep)" rather than as "Subtypes" of Disorders of Arousal. The definitions of Confusional Arousals, Sleepwalking, and Sleep Terrors are virtually identical. The ICSD lists Sleep Related Eating Disorder under “Other Parasomnias” but does not mention “sexsomnia” or “sleepsex”. confusional arousal 327.41, sleep walking 307.46, sleep terrors 307.46.References

1. Bruni O, Ferri R, Novelli L, Finotti E, Miano S, Guilleminault C. NREM Sleep instability in children with  sleep terrors: the role of slow wave activity interruptions. Clinical Neurophysiology 2008 May;119(5):985-92.

2. Mason TBA, 2nd, Pack AI. Pediatric parasomnias. Sleep 2007 Feb1;30 2):141-51.

3. Mahowald MW, Schenck CH. Evolving concepts of human state dissociation. Archives Italiennes de Biologie 2001;139:269-300.

The work group is recommending that this disorder be subsumed into a new disorder: Disorder of Arousal.

Sleep Terror Disorder

A. Recurrent episodes of abrupt awakening from sleep, usually occurring during the first third of the major sleep episode and beginning with a panicky scream.

B. Intense fear and signs of autonomic arousal, such as tachycardia, rapid breathing, and sweating, during each episode.

C. Relative unresponsiveness to efforts of others to comfort the person during the episode.

D. No detailed dream is recalled and there is amnesia for the episode.

E. The episodes cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

F. The disturbance is not due to the direct physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug of abuse, a medication) or a general medical condition.
