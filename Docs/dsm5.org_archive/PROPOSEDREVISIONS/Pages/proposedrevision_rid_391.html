M 03 Obstructive Sleep Apnea Hypopnea Syndrome

Obstructive Sleep Apnea Hypopnea Syndrome

Updated May 1, 2012 

A.          Diagnosis requires Criteria A1 plus A2 together, or A3 alone.

1.      With Criterion A2, symptoms of:

a) Nocturnal breathing disturbances: Snoring, snorting/gasping or breathing pauses during sleep, or

b) Daytime sleepiness, fatigue, or unrefreshing sleep despite sufficient opportunities to sleep that are unexplained by another medical or psychiatric morbidity

2.      Evidence by polysomnography of 5 or more obstructive apneas or hypopneas per hour of sleep ; or

3.      Evidence by polysomnography of 15 more obstructive apneas and/or hypopneas per hour of sleep regardless of accompanying symptoms

 

Note: for diagnostic criteria used in children, symptoms commonly include irritability and behavioral problems; signs may include chest retractions during sleep. The number of apneas/hypopneas supportive of a diagnosis is lower than in adults.

 

Rationale for proposed changes to the Sleep-Related Breathing Disorders in DSM-5

Updated May 1, 2012

 

Summary:  The Sleep Disorders Workgroup recommends two key changes in the Sleep Disordered Breathing diagnostic criteria for consideration in DSM-5:

1)     Identification of specific subtypes and diagnostic criteria for: Obstructive Sleep Apnea Hypopnea; Central Sleep Apnea and Sleep Related Hypoventilation

2)     Inclusion of polysomnographic criteria in the diagnostic criteria

3)     Inclusion of symptoms of nocturnal breathing disturbances in the diagnostic criteria for Obstructive Sleep Apnea Hypopnea.

 

Change # 1: Provide specific diagnostic criteria for: Obstructive Sleep Apnea Hypopnea; Central Sleep Apnea and Sleep Related Hypoventilation rather than use only one set of criteria for “Breathing Related Sleep Disorder.”

Reasons for change: This recommendation is consistent with current understanding that there is a spectrum of Sleep Related Breathing Disorders, ranging from central sleep apnea to obstructive sleep apnea and sleep related hypoventilation, with overlapping as well as unique pathophysiology. The current text provides an overview of these inter-related disorders, but now provides specific definitions for each of these entities.

Evidence for change: The proposed change would align the DSMV with the ICSD. Although these disorders may often share common underlying physiological risk factors (respiratory control instability), physiological and anatomic studies indicate differences in the pathogeneses of these disorders, with central sleep apnea less dependent on structural airway abnormalities as compared to obstructive sleep apnea, which is more dependent on increased upper airway resistance, and sleep related hypoventilation often co-morbid with other disorders that depress ventilation (Dempsey et al., 2006; see p. 50, 57-8; Casey et al., 2007; see p. 1936-7) .  Co-morbidities associated with each condition, and thus, factors influencing clinical recognition, also differ, with obstructive sleep apnea hypopnea highly prevalent in individuals with obesity, hypertension and diabetes; central sleep apnea may be associated with several conditions, including heart failure and chronic opioid use; and sleep related hypoventilation frequently associated with lung disease or neuromuscular or chest wall disorders (Dempsey et al., 2006; see 60-61; Casey et al., 2007; seep. 1936-7).

Change # 2: Inclusion of polysomnographic criteria in the diagnostic criteria

Reasons for change: This change would align the DSMV recommendations with ICSD. Symptoms are not sufficiently specific to diagnose sleep related breathing disturbances.  An abundance of epidemiological data support an association between AHI values and adverse health outcomes.

Evidence for change: Symptom reports are not sufficiently sensitive or specific to diagnose sleep related breathing disorders, although they can be used as screening tools (Young et al., 2002; 1218-19).  AHI levels are linearly associated increased incidence of adverse cardiovascular events. (Young et al., 2002; 1223-27).

Change #3: Inclusion of symptoms of nocturnal breathing disturbances in the diagnostic criteria for Obstructive Sleep Apnea Hypopnea.

Reasons for change: This change would align the DSMV recommendations with ICSD. Nocturnal symptoms reflect the occurrence of breathing disorders during the sleep period.

Evidence for change: Objective measurements of snoring intensity correlate with AHI severity (Maimon et al. 2010). Snoring and gasping contribute significantly to sleep apnea prediction, and in some studies, are the most significant symptoms associated with sleep apnea (Ramachandran et al., 2009; p. 928, 933, 937(Table 1).)

FOSQ-10

This new disorder is not listed in DSM-IV and was previously subsumed under Breathing Related Sleep Disorder.
